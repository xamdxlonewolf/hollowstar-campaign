# Session Zero — The Hollowstar Campaign

## GM Schedule and Reference Document

_3 players, all TTRPG experienced but new to Daggerheart. 3 hours maximum. Character creation happens in session. World presentation is surface-level — the feel of the world, not the plot. Players leave excited, connected to each other, and knowing this world belongs to them._

_This document is yours to have in front of you. It covers the full schedule, the Daggerheart essentials cheat sheet, world presentation notes, and prompts for the collaborative worldbuilding section._

---

## THE SCHEDULE AT A GLANCE

|Block|Time|Content|
|---|---|---|
|Welcome and framing|15 min|Tone, expectations, the kind of campaign this is|
|Daggerheart essentials|30 min|The mechanics they need to know before they build|
|Character creation|60 min|Build in the official tool, class/ancestry, the personal details|
|Connections and collaborative worldbuilding|30 min|How they know each other, homebrew origins if needed|
|World presentation|30 min|Lumencrest — the feel, the look, what's visible|
|Wrap and questions|15 min|Open floor, logistics, hype|

**Total: 3 hours.** Each block has flex built in — if character creation runs long (it will), trim the world presentation slightly. The connections block is the one not to cut — it's what makes session one feel alive.

## BLOCK ONE — WELCOME AND FRAMING

### 15 minutes

This sets the tone for everything that follows. Don't rush it.

**Open with something like this — adapt it to your voice:**

> "Before we build anything, I want to tell you what kind of campaign this is. This is a long one — we're talking potentially a year or more of play. It's political, it's personal, it has genuine stakes, and the world is going to change based on what you do in it. I don't mean that in the 'your choices matter' way that every GM says and then rails you down a track. I mean it literally — the continent at the end of this campaign will be different from the continent at the start, and the difference will be you.
> 
> The tone is grounded. People make hard decisions for real reasons. The villains aren't evil for fun — they're people who decided something was worth doing and were wrong about that in ways that are understandable. There will be moments that are heavy. There will also be moments that are genuinely funny and warm, because that's what it's like when a group of people you care about are doing something together that matters.
> 
> The world has a specific feeling — it's a civilization at a turning point. You'll feel that from the first session. What the turning point is, you'll discover. Some of you will figure it out faster than others depending on your character's background. Nobody knows everything yet, including me — the story we're going to tell together will fill in things I haven't decided."

**Then cover the practical table stuff:**

- Session frequency and length you're planning
- How you handle safety tools — X-card on the table, lines and veils if you've established them, open door policy
- Phones — your preference
- Between-session communication — Discord, group chat, wherever you're coordinating
- Confirm everyone has access to the official Daggerheart tool

**Ask the table:** Does anyone have anything they specifically want to see in this campaign, or anything they'd like to avoid? Take brief notes. This is content, not a lengthy discussion.

## BLOCK TWO — DAGGERHEART ESSENTIALS

### 30 minutes

They have TTRPG experience so you don't need to explain what a d20 is. Focus on what's _different_ about Daggerheart. Keep it conversational — you're not lecturing, you're giving them a map before they enter new territory.

---

### THE CORE MECHANIC — Hope and Fear Dice

**The fundamental difference from D&D/Pathfinder:**

Daggerheart uses two twelve-sided dice simultaneously — one is the **Hope die** and one is the **Fear die**. They're different colors. You roll both at the same time, always.

**Add them together** to get your result, then compare to the Difficulty. But here's what makes it different:

- **If the Hope die is higher** — you succeed with Hope. The GM gains no Fear tokens. Good outcome, possibly something extra.
- **If the Fear die is higher** — even if you succeed, the GM gains a Fear token. Something complicates the situation. You got what you wanted but it cost something, or something else just got worse.
- **If they're equal** — that's a Critical Success. Best possible outcome.

**What this means at the table:** Every roll tells a story beyond pass/fail. A success with Fear isn't a failure — you still succeed — but the world gets a little darker at the same time. Players never feel like the dice are simply blocking them; something always happens.

**Key phrase to remember:** _"You succeed, but..."_ or _"You succeed, and..."_ — the Hope/Fear split determines which version of success you get.

---

### HOPE AND FEAR AS RESOURCES

**Hope tokens (players earn these):**

- Generated by rolling with the Hope die higher
- Spent to activate class abilities, enhance rolls, help other players
- Think of them as momentum — good choices and lucky rolls build them up

**Fear tokens (GM earns these):**

- Generated when the Fear die is higher on any player's roll
- Spent by the GM to have the world push back — enemy actions, complications, environmental pressure, NPC responses
- The GM doesn't roll dice for enemies — they spend Fear instead

**What this means at the table:** The GM's power to make things difficult is directly tied to how the players' rolls go. A run of bad luck generates a run of GM pressure. A run of good luck depletes it. The tension is mechanical, not adversarial.

---

### THE ACTION SYSTEM

**Three types of rolls:**

**Action Rolls** — when you do something with an uncertain outcome. Roll Hope + Fear dice, add the relevant trait modifier, compare to Difficulty (set by GM, usually 10-17 depending on complexity).

**Reaction Rolls** — when the world acts on you and you respond. Same mechanic, different framing.

**No roll needed** — if something is clearly within a character's capability and the stakes don't require uncertainty, the GM can simply say it works. Don't roll for everything.

**The six traits** (what you add to rolls):

- **Agility** — speed, stealth, precision movement
- **Strength** — force, endurance, physical power
- **Finesse** — dexterity, subtlety, careful manipulation
- **Instinct** — awareness, reaction, reading situations
- **Presence** — charisma, leadership, social influence
- **Knowledge** — memory, analysis, technical expertise

Each character has modifiers for each trait ranging from -1 to +4 at creation.

---

### DISTANCE (NOT FEET)

Daggerheart doesn't use a grid or specific distances in feet. It uses five range bands:

|Range|What It Means|
|---|---|
|**Melee**|Right next to you, arm's reach|
|**Close**|A few steps away, same room roughly|
|**Near**|Across the room, short sprint|
|**Far**|Across a large space, requires real movement|
|**Very Far**|At the edge of perception, long-range only|

**Why this works:** It keeps combat narrative rather than tactical. Movement becomes a story decision rather than a grid calculation. "I move Close to the guard" rather than "I move 15 feet." This takes a session or two to feel natural but makes the game flow faster.

---

### HIT POINTS AND STRESS

Characters have **Hit Points** (physical harm) and **Stress** (mental/emotional strain).

**Hit Points:** Standard damage reduces HP. At zero you take a **Death Move** — roll and see what happens. It isn't automatic death; Daggerheart treats character death as a narrative event rather than a mechanical one.

**Stress:** Certain actions, failures, and situations add Stress. At maximum Stress, characters become less effective — some abilities require spending Stress, creating a resource tension. Stress clears on rest.

**Why Stress matters for this campaign:** A lot of what players will encounter in Lumencrest is the kind of thing that accumulates as Stress — moral weight, political pressure, discovering things they'd rather not know. The mechanical Stress system will feel very natural for the tone.

---

### COMBAT — THE QUICK VERSION

- **Player turn:** Describe what you're doing, make an Action Roll if needed, deal damage on success
- **Enemy actions:** GM spends Fear to activate — enemies don't roll, they simply act when the GM spends the resource
- **Conditions:** Applied by specific attacks or situations — see the Conditions Reference if relevant (you have [[Lumencrest_Conditions_Reference]] for campaign-specific ones)
- **Advantage/Disadvantage:** Some situations or abilities grant these — roll an additional die of the relevant type and take the better result

**Key difference from D&D:** Initiative is looser. Players generally act in the order they want to, with the GM spending Fear to interject enemy actions. Combat is faster and more collaborative as a result.

---

### ANCESTRY AND CLASS — BRIEF OVERVIEW

**Ancestry** — what your character is (human, elf, dwarf, etc.). Gives one passive trait and shapes roleplay.

**Class** — what your character does (warrior, rogue, mage equivalents, etc.). Gives your primary abilities, your Hope and Fear die sizes, and your stat spread.

**The card system:** Abilities are drawn from a card deck for each class — you build a hand of abilities rather than picking from a static list. This means two characters of the same class can feel very different depending on their draws and choices. You'll see this in the character creation tool.

**Subclass:** Chosen at character creation, refines the class further. This is where a lot of the character identity comes from.

---

### QUESTIONS?

Take a few minutes here for questions on the mechanics. Keep it moving — they'll learn most of this by doing. The goal is orientation, not mastery.

**Reassurance to give:** "You're not going to get this wrong. The system is very forgiving of learning-curve moments and I'll prompt you when a roll is needed and what trait to use. By session three it'll feel completely natural."

---

## BLOCK THREE — CHARACTER CREATION

### 60 minutes (this block will try to run long — hold the time)

Direct everyone to the official Daggerheart character creation tool. Walk through it together rather than splitting off — questions will come up and shared answers save time.

**Walk through in this order:**

---

**Step 1 — Ancestry (5 minutes)**

Pick ancestry. It gives one passive trait. Key question to ask the table as they pick:

_"Where are you from? Not your ancestry — your actual place. A city, a village, a ship, somewhere. This doesn't have to be Lumencrest. It can be anywhere in the world."_

Note what each player says. This feeds directly into the worldbuilding block.

---

**Step 2 — Class and Subclass (10 minutes)**

Pick class and subclass. Walk through each class briefly if people are uncertain. Questions to ask as they decide:

_"What does your character do when things go wrong? That's usually your class."_ _"What does your character care about most? That's usually your subclass."_

---

**Step 3 — Stats and Abilities (15 minutes)**

Assign stat modifiers, draw ability cards (the tool handles this), pick starting abilities. Let them read their abilities — this is where excitement builds as people see what their character can actually do.

**GM note:** Watch for combinations that seem like they'll be fun at the table. Note them. You'll want to create situations that let those abilities shine early in Act One.

---

**Step 4 — The Personal Details (20 minutes)**

This is the part of Daggerheart character creation that other systems don't have, and it's the most important part for session zero. The tool walks through these but make sure each player answers them at the table, out loud, so everyone hears:

- **Who do you care about most?** (An NPC in their life — not a party member yet)
- **What's the most valuable thing you own?**
- **What do you struggle with?**
- **What's your relationship with hope?** (Optimist, cynic, something in between)
- **What would make you compromise your values?**

**Why out loud:** These answers tell the other players who their character is before the connections are made. They also tell you, as GM, what levers to pull. Write them down.

---

**Step 5 — Background and Appearance (10 minutes)**

Name, appearance, brief background. Encourage specificity over completeness — "a Shadowside-born human who left at sixteen" is more useful than a detailed backstory nobody will remember. Three sentences maximum.

---

## BLOCK FOUR — CONNECTIONS AND COLLABORATIVE WORLDBUILDING

### 30 minutes — DO NOT CUT THIS

This is where the campaign actually starts. Everything before this was preparation; this is the table creating something together.

---

### PART A — HOW DO YOU KNOW EACH OTHER? (15 minutes)

Daggerheart has a formal Connections system — characters have established relationships before play begins. Work through this with the table.

**The framing:**

> "These three people are together at the start of the campaign. They're in the same place, moving toward the same situation. How do they know each other? You don't have to be friends — you just have to have a history. Adversarial history counts. Complicated history is better than simple history."

**Prompt the table with these questions if they get stuck:**

- _"Have two of you worked together before? What was the job?"_
- _"Does anyone owe anyone else something?"_
- _"Has anyone met before under circumstances they'd rather forget?"_
- _"Is anyone here because someone else asked them to be?"_

Let this evolve organically. Your job is to facilitate, not direct. Take notes on what they establish — these become campaign threads you can pull on.

**The goal:** Each character should have at least one established connection to at least one other character. A three-person party ideally has a triangle — everyone connected to everyone in some way, even if some connections are indirect.

---

### PART B — COLLABORATIVE WORLDBUILDING (15 minutes)

This is where you give the players ownership of the world.

**Open with:**

> "This world has a lot of places in it. Some of them I've built in detail. Some of them are spaces I've left open deliberately because I knew you'd fill them better than I would. If your character is from somewhere that isn't Lumencrest — somewhere I haven't detailed — we can build it right now, together. Small or large, whatever you need."

**For each player whose character is from outside Lumencrest:**

Ask these questions and write down the answers:

1. _"What's the name of the place?"_
2. _"What's one thing everyone from there knows that outsiders don't?"_
3. _"What's the one thing your character is glad to have left behind — and the one thing they miss?"_
4. _"Is there anyone there who matters to your character now — someone who might send a letter, or need help, or cause a problem?"_

Three to four questions per player, five minutes maximum per player. You're not building a full location — you're establishing enough that the place feels real when it comes up.

**For players whose characters are from Lumencrest:**

Ask these instead:

1. _"Which district? You know what it looks, sounds, and smells like — what's the first specific thing that comes to mind?"_
2. _"What does your character think of the city? Home, cage, both, neither?"_
3. _"Is there a specific person in the city you have history with — someone who's still there?"_

**Close the worldbuilding section with:**

> "Everything you just told me is real. It's in the world now. If it becomes relevant to the story, we'll use it. If it doesn't, it's still there — it's just the parts of the world we haven't visited yet."

---

## BLOCK FIVE — WORLD PRESENTATION

### 30 minutes

You're not revealing plot. You're giving them the feel of Lumencrest — enough that they can picture it, smell it, hear it, and orient themselves when session one begins. Keep it sensory and specific. The details that land best are small concrete ones, not sweeping descriptions.

---

### THE OPENING (5 minutes)

Read this or adapt it in your voice. This is the first time the world speaks:

> "Lumencrest is the most impressive thing on the continent. That's not an opinion — it's the first thing everyone agrees on when they see it, regardless of what they think of the place afterward. At night, from the last ridge before the valley, it glows. Not with fire. With electricity — a cold, steady white light that doesn't flicker, doesn't dim, doesn't behave like fire behaves. The Lumen Network runs through every wall and under every street, and the Heart beneath the city has been feeding it for twelve centuries.
> 
> The cable cars run overhead on four lines, moving at the same steady speed day and night. There's a hum — everywhere, all the time, in the walls, under your feet, in the slight vibration of anything attached to the Network. After a week you stop hearing it. After a month you'd notice its absence more than its presence.
> 
> It's a city of 80,000 people. It has every kind of person in it. It has the best food on the continent in a Halfling Quarter that feeds the rest of the city every morning before dawn. It has a political class that has been gridlocked for three years. It has a district that has never had electricity and has been waiting for it for six centuries. It has 2,000 mechanical constructs called Clanks who are legally indentured workers and are, depending on who you ask, either a labor system or something that doesn't have a polite name yet.
> 
> It is not a perfect city. It is a real one."

---

### DISTRICT OVERVIEW (10 minutes)

Give each district one or two sentences — feel, not detail. This is orientation, not a tour. Use the sensory details we know.

**The Underground and Shadowside:** _"The southern edge of the city has no electricity. It never has. The Night Market runs after dark on oil lamps and coal fires, and it sells things you can't buy anywhere with a city license. It's not dangerous if you know how to be there. Most people from outside the city don't."_

**The Manufactories:** _"The northern district is where the city's labor force lives and works. Factory floors the size of city blocks, chimney stacks, the specific smell of hot metal and coal particulate. The workers here are organized in a way that makes the Council nervous, and they have reason to be organized."_

**The Halfling Quarter:** _"West of the Manufactories, lower buildings, brewery towers, the smell of yeast and fresh bread at all hours. The Quarter feeds the city. It's the warmest district in Lumencrest and the one most likely to tell you what it actually thinks."_

**Lamplight Ward:** _"The commercial center. Electric amber street lamps, the cable cars overhead, Merchant Square with its fountain always running. The Theatre, the entertainment quarter, the Night Pharmacy that never closes. This is where the city's professional and merchant class lives — prosperous, and right now beginning to worry."_

**The Academic Quarter:** _"The Luminarium — the city's research institution — occupies its own district. Smart people working very long hours on problems the rest of the city doesn't fully understand. The lights there are on at midnight. They have been for a while now."_

**The Radiant Core:** _"The political center. The Accord Chamber where the Council meets. The estates of the city's old families. Wide streets, full electric brightness, guards at every significant intersection. The city looks the most like itself here. The cracks are less visible."_

**The Vault District:** _"There is a wall in the center of the city. Inside the wall is the Vault District. You don't go in without authorization. The lights inside never dim."_

---

### WHAT PLAYERS CAN KNOW FROM THEIR BACKGROUNDS (10 minutes)

**For characters from Lumencrest:** Take a few minutes individually or as a group to add detail to their specific district. What does a Shadowside native know about the Night Market that a visitor doesn't? What does a Halfling Quarter native think of when they smell yeast? Keep it personal and specific.

**For characters from outside Lumencrest:** Give them the visitor's first impression — the light on the horizon at night, the gate, the hum. They don't know the district details yet. They'll learn them.

**What nobody knows yet (GM note):** Don't mention the power crisis, the Heart's decline, the brownouts, the classified research, the disappearances, the factions' political struggle, or anything that belongs to the plot. That's for session one and beyond. If a player asks about the brownouts or the political situation, it's fine to say _"You've heard vague things — rumors that the power rationing has gotten worse recently — but you don't have details. That's something you'll learn when you arrive."_

---

### THE CLANKS — WORTH ADDRESSING (5 minutes)

This is worth raising explicitly because it's going to come up in session one and some players may not have context.

> "One thing worth knowing before we play — there are approximately 2,000 Clanks in Lumencrest. They're mechanical constructs, built and maintained by the Luminarium, and they serve labor indenture terms — usually fifteen years of assigned work in exchange for a life in the city afterward. They're people. They have preferences, relationships, opinions about their situation. Whether their situation is acceptable is one of the things this city actively argues about. You're going to encounter them. I want you to know going in that they're not background furniture."

---

## BLOCK SIX — WRAP AND QUESTIONS

### 15 minutes

**Open the floor:**

> "That's the world as you know it going in. Session one, you'll be in Lumencrest on your first full day in the city. Something will happen. What you do with it is yours."

**Questions to invite:**

- Any questions about the world or the city?
- Any questions about the system we didn't cover?
- Any last adjustments to characters based on what we discussed?

**Logistics confirmation:**

- Next session date and time
- Where you're playing / what platform if online
- Where to find the Daggerheart rules reference (official site)
- Where to find your campaign materials if you're sharing any (Obsidian, Notion, a shared folder — wherever you're hosting the documents)

**Close with something like this:**

> "One last thing. This campaign is going to be long and it's going to matter. The decisions you make — who you help, who you don't, what you decide is worth fighting for — those decisions are going to shape what this world becomes. I mean that literally. So come to session one ready to be in it. We start with a city that looks like it's working. Pay attention to the parts that don't."

### ROLL RESULTS

|Result|Meaning|
|---|---|
|Critical Success (both dice equal)|Best possible outcome, GM gains nothing|
|Success with Hope (Hope die higher)|You succeed, GM gains no Fear|
|Success with Fear (Fear die higher)|You succeed, GM gains 1 Fear token|
|Failure with Hope (total below DC, Hope higher)|You fail, but something small goes your way|
|Failure with Fear (total below DC, Fear higher)|You fail, GM gains 1 Fear token|

### THE SIX TRAITS

|Trait|Use For|
|---|---|

|Strength|Force, breaking things, physical endurance|
|Finesse|Delicate work, sleight of hand, careful manipulation|
|Instinct|Awareness, gut feelings, reading danger|

|Knowledge|Recall, analysis, technical expertise, history|

---

### DISTANCE BANDS

**Melee → Close → Near → Far → Very Far**

Moving one band costs your movement. Some abilities specify range. If in doubt: can you reach out and touch them? Melee. Same room? Close to Near. Different end of a large space? Far.

---

### HOPE AND FEAR ECONOMY

**Players spend Hope to:** Activate class abilities, enhance rolls, assist allies, activate specific card effects.

**GM spends Fear to:** Activate enemy actions, introduce complications, trigger environmental effects, escalate stakes.

**Fear is not punishment** — it's the world responding. Players generating Fear through their rolls isn't failure; it's the natural rhythm of the system.

---

### CONDITIONS (QUICK REFERENCE)

Standard Daggerheart conditions:

- **Restrained** — cannot move
- **Vulnerable** — damage bypasses armor, hits HP directly
- **Frightened** — must move away from fear source, rolls against it made at disadvantage
- **Hidden** — cannot be directly targeted, ends when position is revealed

Campaign-specific conditions are in [[Lumencrest_Conditions_Reference]].

### IF A PLAYER ASKS ABOUT SOMETHING NOT COVERED HERE

The official Daggerheart rules are at **daggerheart.com** (or through the Darrington Press site). The core rulebook PDF is the authoritative source. For session zero and session one, the above covers everything they'll need. Edge cases can be looked up as they arise — Daggerheart is designed to run smoothly on rulings rather than stopping for rules research.
