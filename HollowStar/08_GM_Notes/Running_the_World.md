# Running the World — GM Philosophy & Guidance

This file is for you, the GM. It covers how to handle this setting's big questions, its tone, its mysteries, and the moments where players will look to you for answers that the world itself doesn't know yet.

---

## WHAT THIS WORLD IS ABOUT

At its core, Hollowstars is about **people making decisions under pressure they didn't ask for and can't fully understand**. The crisis is real. The stakes are real. Nobody is the villain of their own story. The world isn't ending — it's changing — and the question is whether that change destroys what people have built or becomes the foundation for something new.

**The tone sits here:**
- Cosmic Horror: 2/10 — the unknown is mysterious and vast, not sanity-breaking
- Political Intrigue: 7/10 — factions have real competing interests and no clean answers
- Hope: 5/10 — things can go badly, things can go well, player choices genuinely matter
- Wonder: 8/10 — the world has beautiful impossible things in it, and those should feel beautiful

**What this is not:** A grimdark setting where everything fails and everyone dies. Nor is it a heroic fantasy where the good guys win because they're good. It's a setting where capable, well-meaning people face a crisis larger than any of them, and what they do about it matters — but doesn't guarantee the outcome they want.

## THE THREE MYSTERIES — AND HOW TO HANDLE THEM

There are three questions at the heart of the setting that have no canonical answer — and they don't need one before you run. These are living mysteries. The options below are the most compelling possibilities based on what's written in the vault, but the truth may be a combination, may emerge from play, or may remain genuinely unknown even by the end of a campaign. That's fine. Present each mystery as exactly what it is: a question the world is asking.

---

### Mystery 1: What Are the Hollowstars, Actually?

**What everyone thinks:** Fallen stars, cosmic debris, divine gifts, tests of faith.

**The options (some possibilities, combinable, and none are required to be the full truth):**

**Option A — The Pruning Mechanism (Sira Vantholm's theory)**
The stars are part of a cycle built into the world's astronomy by the pre-Hollostar civilization — or something older. They fall on a schedule. They don't intend harm any more than winter intends to kill crops. The question isn't why they fall, but what the surviving civilizations are supposed to do with the interval between falls. The pre-Hollostar ruins aren't warnings — they're *instructions*.

*Good for:* Campaigns focused on deciphering ancient knowledge, races to understand before impact, bittersweet acceptance of the cycle.

**Option B — The Hunger**
The Hollowstars are drawn to the world by something the world generates — accumulated civilization, concentrated life, Hollostar energy from previous falls. The world itself attracts them. The falling is self-perpetuating: each impact leaves fragments that power civilization, which generates energy, which attracts the next star. The world is, in some sense, a trap.

*Good for:* Moral complexity around whether civilization itself is the problem, whether fragments should be used at all, whether the world can be "quieted."

**Option C — The Divine Mechanism**
The radical Hexarch sect is correct: the Hollowstars *are* the gods, or are the physical form of divine action. Each fall is an act of will, not physics. The question is what the will wants — and whether it's possible to communicate with it, bargain with it, or resist it.

*Good for:* Campaigns where theological conflict has cosmic stakes, where faith can be tested and vindicated or shattered.

**Option D — Unknown Origin, Knowable Effect**
The Hollowstars' origin is genuinely unknowable with current tools. What can be known is their effect — and the pre-Hollostar civilization understood the effects well enough to survive multiple cycles. The mystery of origin becomes irrelevant when you understand how to survive.

*Good for:* Pragmatic campaigns, players who prefer tangible problems over metaphysical ones, the Somnarch approach.

**Option E — The Response**
The Hollowstars are not attracted to power or energy — they are attracted to *need*. The world calls, and the stars answer. Each fall has given exactly what a place and its people desperately lacked.

The second fall struck a settlement of scholars in a river valley who had the knowledge and community to build civilization but no power to sustain it. The Heart gave them *power* — literal electrical energy that made everything they wanted to build possible.

The third fall struck an empty desert with no reason to sustain human life. The Endless Eye gave *water* — an impossible freshwater lake in the middle of arid wasteland, the one thing that made community there viable at all.

**What does Frostwell need?**

This is the question players should eventually be asking. Read the region carefully before deciding — the answer is embedded in who Frostwell is, not what it lacks materially.

Frostwell is a land that *preserves*. The permafrost holds pre-Hollostar ruins intact for 10,000 years. The Ember Cradle's warmth has been building for 30 years. The Nine Hearths culture is built entirely on memory as survival — oral traditions, route knowledge, winter wisdom passed down because forgetting kills. The Drakona came here specifically because something in this place holds their origin.

But Frostwell is *fracturing*. The Stayer/Leaver split has torn apart a culture whose deepest value is the hearth — fire as life, collective warmth, the sacred obligation to maintain what keeps everyone alive together. The Nine Hearths' most fundamental identity is community gathered around shared warmth, and that community is shattering precisely as the star approaches.

The fourth fall's gift may not be heat, power, or water. Frostwell has cold and isolation in abundance — what it is losing is *connection*. Its people are scattering. Its knowledge is fragmenting. Its ancient preserved wisdom is being abandoned along with the evacuating villages.

**What the fourth fall gives Frostwell:** Something that allows what was preserved here — the ruins, the knowledge, the Drakona's spiritual purpose, the Nine Hearths' oral memory — to survive the impact and remain connected rather than scattered. Not warmth as heat. Warmth as *bond*.

What that looks like physically is yours to decide:
- A network of geothermal warmth across the tundra that makes the transformed post-impact region habitable in a new way
- Something from the sealed Ember Cradle chamber that awakens and becomes a new center of community
- The Frostvault's ancient observatory becomes something new — a place where the preserved knowledge of 10,000 years becomes accessible to the world that survives

**The theological implication of Option E:** The stars are not indifferent physics, not divine punishment, not attracted to accumulated power. They respond to *genuine need* — the kind that a people cannot solve for themselves. This means the crisis is also, in some sense, a recognition. Frostwell is worth answering. The question players may eventually ask: if the stars respond to need, what did Frostwell do to be heard?

**The complication:** The Merchant Circle would interpret Option E as meaning the Endless Eye's decline is the star withdrawing because the need has been *met* — the desert has water now, the community exists. This is horrifying to the people of the Basin and demands an answer: has the Eye's need been fully met, or is the star leaving too soon?

*Good for:* Campaigns where players want to understand *why* rather than just *what*, theological and emotional depth, making the fourth fall feel like recognition rather than catastrophe.*

> **Note — The Definitive Version:** A complete cosmology document has been developed that synthesizes and resolves the options above. If you want the full answer rather than working from options, see [[08_GM_Notes/The_Hollow_Truth|The Hollow Truth]]. It is built on Option E's emotional framework but answers the questions Option E leaves open: what exactly is inside the stars, why the Heart produces electricity and the Eye produces water, why both are dying, and what the fourth fall's contact will actually be. The options above remain useful for GMs who want to leave more unresolved.

### Mystery 2: What Did Thessara See at the Third Fall's Impact Site?

Elder Thessara Windroot was there when the Endless Eye formed. She saw something emerge from the crater in the first hours. She's been deciding for 700 years whether to describe it. The fourth fall is making her decide: yes.

**What she saw — options:**

**Option A:** A figure. Humanoid, enormous, made of light and crushed stone, that stood at the center of the impact zone for approximately one hour and then was gone. The Endless Eye formed around where it stood. Whether it was a god, a projection, a reaction, or something else, she doesn't know. But it looked at her. She is certain of that.

**Option B:** The crater wasn't empty. The star didn't vaporize on impact — it *opened*. Like an egg hatching. Whatever was inside was already gone by the time anyone else arrived. She doesn't know what it was. She knows what the shell looked like, and the shell had markings identical to the Spine Stones currently rising on the Isle of the Broken Sphere.

**Option C:** Nothing emerged. What she saw was the *absence* of something — a perfect spherical void at the center of the impact, roughly 100 feet across, that persisted for three hours and then filled with water from nowhere. The Endless Eye didn't form naturally. The water came from somewhere else entirely.

### Mystery 3: What Is the Sleeper?

Something vast beneath the Isle of the Broken Sphere is waking. It has been waking for thirty years. The Spine Stones have pre-Hollostar markings. The forest is dying outward from the fault line. Elder Tess can hear something.

**Options:**

**Option A — Surviving Entity**
The pre-Hollostar civilization didn't all die. One of them — one very old, very patient thing — retreated beneath the island during the first fall and has been sleeping since. It is waking because it senses the fourth fall approaching. It is neither benevolent nor malevolent; it is something so old that human moral categories don't fully apply. It has things to say. Whether anyone will understand them is the question.

**Option B — The Star Fragment**
The Isle sits atop a buried piece of the first Hollostar — not a fragment, but a significant piece. Large enough that the fourth fall's approach is resonating with it, waking energy that has been dormant for 10,000 years. The Spine Stones are the fragment's energy crystallizing into the rock above it. What happens when the fourth fall arrives and two Hollowstars' energy interact simultaneously is unknown.

**Option C — The World's Answer**
The world has a form of consciousness — distributed, slow, not quite person-shaped — and the repeated Hollostar impacts have been damaging it. What's waking on the Isle is not a creature or a fragment but the world itself becoming aware enough, in one location, to respond. The Spine Stone markings are not pre-Hollostar writing. They are the world writing its own language for the first time.

## HOW TO RUN THE FACTIONS

**The most important principle:** Every faction leader believes they are doing the right thing. Nobody is running a villain faction. The Four Houses are protecting their families. The Merchant Circle is keeping people alive in a desert. Lumencrest's Expansionists genuinely believe seizing the Endless Eye saves more lives than it harms. Let players encounter this complexity rather than resolving it for them.

**Factions should act whether or not players engage.** If the party spends three sessions in the Northern Expanse, Lumencrest's Council is still making decisions, the Green Covenant is still managing its Thorn problem, the Tide King is still extending his network. At the start of each session block, ask yourself: what has each relevant faction done since the party last touched them?

**Faction reputation is a resource.** Being known as allies of the Somnarchs opens doors with the Green Covenant and First Flame but creates friction with the Hexarchs and Lumencrest Conservators. Being known as Lumencrest agents closes Green Covenant doors entirely. Track this informally — players should feel their relationships with factions have weight.

**The Sixth Seat is a slow-burn political engine.** The Rivermark Sovereignty's campaign for Accord membership can run in the background for an entire campaign, with players occasionally encountering its effects. Mirelen Starweaver's undisclosed Merchant Circle offer is the trigger that could end the campaign — if players learn about it, they have to decide what to do.

## HOW TO RUN THE COUNTDOWN

The fourth fall is approaching. Everything the players do happens in the shadow of this. But the countdown should feel like pressure, not determinism.

**Key principle: The fall happens regardless.** Players cannot stop the Hollostar. They can influence where exactly it lands (unlikely but possible), whether communities survive it, whether factions are positioned to rebuild, and what the world looks like after. Their agency is in the aftermath, not the prevention.

**The fall should not happen in session 1.** The ideal campaign arc has the fall in the final third — after players have built relationships, after the stakes are personal, after they have things to lose. Fourteen months of in-world time gives you plenty of room.

**Use the approach as pressure, not pacing.** Each session doesn't need to advance the countdown. But every few sessions something should change — Lumencrest's Heart dims further, a Frostwell community that chose to stay goes silent, a Seeker expedition returns with disturbing data. The world is moving.

## TONE TOOLS

**When to lean into wonder:** Ancient ruins, intact Hollostar sites, the Glittering Marshes at night, Cindrath's caldera, the Endless Eye at depth, the first time players see the approaching star visible in daylight. These moments should feel genuinely beautiful and genuinely strange. Spend time on description.

**When to lean into weight:** Refugee camps, communities making impossible choices, faction leaders who are clearly doing their best and clearly not enough, the moment players realize a secret changes everything they thought was true. Don't rescue these moments with humor or convenient solutions. Let them sit.

**When to lean into humor:** Katari oral-calendar recitations taken literally, Clanks encountering biological needs they don't have, dwarven Deep Reckoning time that doesn't sync with anything, the social collision between elven timescale and human urgency. This world has comedy in it. Use it.

**The Darkness is not a monster.** The Darkness is the accumulated weight of things failing and people being frightened. When it manifests in play, it should feel like dread, not encounter. Transformed creatures are symptoms, not causes. Power failures in Lumencrest at night are Darkness. The look on Elowen's face when he realizes he's making decisions too slowly again is Darkness.

## THINGS THAT SHOULD SURPRISE PLAYERS

Save these for the right moment:

- **Vaela's fifth fall calculation.** Not to immediately terrify — to reframe. Surviving the fourth fall might be preparation for something else. Give this only to players who've earned it through Somnarch engagement.

- **Thessara's account of the third impact.** Whatever version you chose, let it contradict something every faction believes. The moment players realize the Endless Eye formed differently than anyone thinks is a campaign pivot.

- **Denn Harrowfield's theory.** When he finally shares what the Hollowstars actually are, it should make players look at every previous session differently. Time this for mid-to-late campaign.

- **Voss's family history.** He left for a reason. Two people knew why, and both are dead. The reason connects to current events in a way he hasn't pieced together yet. When he does piece it together — in front of players who've been traveling with him — that's a character moment worth the long setup.

- **The sealed chamber beneath Ember Cradle.** Deep-Keeper Varra knows what's inside. The seals are softening. What's in there is one of the setting's most significant reveals — tie it directly to whichever "what are the Hollowstars" answer you chose.

## THE WORLD AFTER THE FALL

You don't need to plan this in detail before the campaign. But have a rough sense of it, because players will start asking.

**What definitely changes:**
- Frostwell Tundra is transformed — geography, climate, access
- The existing Hollostar sites (Heart, Endless Eye) respond to the impact in some way
- Every ancient site on the continent activates simultaneously on impact day
- The political landscape shifts dramatically based on who survived, who prepared, who helped others

**What players should be able to influence:**
- Whether Lumencrest is warned about the Heart failure in time to prepare
- Whether the Frostwell communities evacuate or stay
- Whether the Somnarchs' knowledge survives and reaches the right people
- Whether the major factions are positioned to cooperate or compete in the aftermath
- Whether Thessara's account of the third fall gets out before the fourth

**The best ending is not "we stopped it."** The best ending is "we made sure something worth rebuilding survived."
