# The Hollowstar Campaign — Full Arc Overview

**Estimated Length:** 80-130 sessions depending on player engagement and exploration depth  
**Tone:** Political intrigue, expedition discovery, human cost, mythological revelation  
**Central Question:** What are Hollowstars — and what does it mean that one is coming home?

---

## The World's Hidden Truth

*GM Only — do not share with players until Act Three*

The Hollowstars are not rocks. They are not random cosmic debris. They are a civilization — the civilization that built the pre-Hollostar ruins beneath Lumencrest, constructed Frostvault, raised the Sleeping Gate, and carved the Shattered Spire. When their world ended — how is unknown, possibly their own doing — they converted themselves entirely into pure energy and launched themselves into the cosmos as stars, carrying everything they were into the void.

They always intended to return. And when they return, they can only do one thing: give. They give what the place they land *needs most*. Lumencrest received power. The Endless Eye received water and life in a desert. The Hollostar falling toward Frostwell will give warmth — potentially transforming the tundra into something habitable over generations.

They are not weapons. They are not disasters. They are a people coming home to a world that has forgotten them entirely — and arriving as gifts to a civilization that doesn't know to be grateful.

The players will not know this at the start. They will piece it together across Act Two. They will confirm it in Frostvault at the start of Act Three. And then they will have to decide what to do with that knowledge — because other people are trying to *redirect* the Hollostar, and redirecting it may be redirecting a *person*.

---

## The Villain Ecosystem

### House Vael
**The Long Game**

Three generations ago the Vaels recognized that Lumencrest — not Montauseau — was the true center of continental power. They established a presence in Lumencrest while maintaining their Empire roots and connections. They are now the most strategically positioned family on the continent: Empire-connected, Council-embedded, patient beyond what most people are capable of.

**Sevan Vael** (human, 54) — House patriarch, Council member (Conservator-aligned independent, eleven years seated). Publicly: a moderate, reasonable voice of stability and thoughtful governance. Privately: a man who has spent three decades positioning his family to catch Lumencrest when it falls. He does not know the full extent of what his daughter is doing. He suspects enough to know he doesn't want the details. If it backfires catastrophically he will not hesitate to sacrifice her to protect the House.

**Lyra Vael** (human, 25) — Sevan's daughter and the operational mind behind the House's darker arrangements. Publicly: a junior House representative, occasionally visible at Council social events, charming, ambitious in ways the elite find admirable. Privately: the architect of the Unseen Guild arrangement and the shard experiment funding. She views morality as a framework other people use to limit themselves. She has identified what the next source of power will be — not political, not economic, but *physical* — and she is building toward it without her father's knowledge or permission. She and the Unseen Guild's leader understand each other completely.

**The House's Strategy:** When Lumencrest fractures — and they believe it will, they have been planning around this for a decade — the Vaels want to be the stabilizing force that steps in. Through Sevan on the Council, through financial leverage over manufacturing guilds that owe them debts, through Empire connections that could provide military "support" if required. They want to catch Lumencrest when it falls and decide what it becomes next.

**What makes them dangerous:** They are patient. They have been building this for thirty years across three generations. They will not expose themselves until they are certain they cannot be stopped.

---

### The Green Covenant Radical Wing
**The Burning Branch**

The Green Covenant's mainstream position is clear: the forest is sacred, Lumencrest's expansion is unacceptable, the Hollostar-dependent civilization is an affront to natural order. Most Covenant members express this through protest, negotiation, and passive resistance. A fringe group — roughly forty members operating in secret cells — has concluded that words accomplish nothing against a civilization with auto-turrets on its walls.

**Their plan:** Target Lumen Network distribution hubs in Lumencrest. Cause power failures. Destabilize the city's political situation. Prevent Lumencrest from mounting effective operations into the Verdant Crescent's timber resources. They are willing to accept civilian casualties as a cost of achieving their goals — they have told themselves the long-term lives saved by protecting the forest justify the short-term deaths caused by their actions.

**Their grievances are legitimate.** This is important. Lumencrest is exploiting the forest. The mainstream Covenant has been ineffective. The radical wing formed because nothing else was working. Players who look closely will find the roots of genuine injustice underneath the violence.

**Key NPCs:**

**Wren** (elf, 134, female) — the radical wing's operational leader. Has been in Lumencrest for six months preparing the network attack. Calm, methodical, has convinced herself that what she's doing is necessary. Has not personally witnessed the consequences of a bombing she planned. This is about to change.

**Ash** (human, 29, male) — Wren's second. Younger, less certain, beginning to have doubts he hasn't voiced. Potentially reachable by players who engage with him honestly.

**Thornwall** (Simiah, age unknown) — the radical wing's contact in the Heartgrove. Does not get hands dirty directly. Provides funding and intelligence. The Archcovenant does not know this person exists within her organization.

---

### The Unseen Guild — Shard Division
**The Experiment**

The Unseen Guild's Lumencrest operation has always been standard: blackmail, coercion, assassination, protection arrangements. The Shard Division is something new — Lyra Vael's investment, the Guild leader's ambition, and a corrupted faction of Starfall Seekers' data all converging into something genuinely monstrous.

**The operation:** Systematic kidnapping of vulnerable people — Frostwell refugees in transit through the Northern Expanse, Shadowside residents whom the Council doesn't count — and exposure to Hollostar shard fragments under controlled conditions. The goal: determine whether shard transformation varies by ancestry, whether it can be directed or controlled, and whether the results can be weaponized into enhanced Guild operatives.

**What they've found so far:** Shard exposure is lethal at most doses for most subjects. A small percentage survive transformed — some usefully, some in ways that are being contained rather than utilized. The data is significant. The Starfall Seeker contacts are very interested in it. Lyra Vael is very interested in the enhancement applications. The Guild leader is very interested in what an army of shard-enhanced operatives could accomplish.

**The Starfall Seeker connection:** Three specific Seeker researchers — not the organization, three individuals who crossed an ethical line — provide shard samples in exchange for the Guild's experimental data. The Seekers want to understand shard effects on living beings. The Guild is doing the experimentation they cannot officially perform. Both sides pretend the arrangement doesn't exist.

**Key NPCs:**

**Mira Voss** (half-elf, 41, female) — Guild leader for Lumencrest and surrounding operations. Brilliant, cold, entirely transactional. Views the shard experiments as a business investment. Has never been in the same room as a subject. Lyra Vael is her most important patron and the closest thing she has to a genuine peer relationship.

**The Collector** (ancestry unknown, deliberately obscured) — the person who actually runs the kidnapping operation. Identity is a secret even within the Guild. Known only by title. Has been operating for two years without being identified. Knows Shadowside better than city watch does.

**Seeker-Researcher Aldis Crane** (human, 52) — the most senior of the three corrupted Starfall Seekers. Genuinely believes the data justifies the method. Has been telling himself this for eighteen months and is beginning to find it harder to believe.

---

### House Caldren — The Isle Gambit
**Silverbelt Coast Political Faction**

House Caldren is in a succession crisis — the main line is disputed, resources are depleted from the Unification Wars era debts, and House Vethara is positioning to absorb their territory when the succession resolves. In desperation, a faction within House Caldren has identified the Isle of the Broken Sphere as a potential salvation: if they can establish a legal claim to the Isle and develop it as a resource, they can rebuild their financial position before Vethara moves against them.

They don't know it's a Dragon Turtle. Nobody does. They've sent survey teams. The survey teams came back with reports of unusual geological activity, valuable mineral deposits in the Spine Stones, and strange warmth that could indicate geothermal energy. They think they've found a prize. They are in the process of establishing a legal framework for claiming the Isle under Silverbelt maritime law.

The Galapa who live there are being classified as "transient occupants" in House Caldren's legal documentation — not citizens with rights, not a permanent community with a claim. Elder Tess knows something is being planned. She doesn't know the specifics yet.

**Key NPCs:**

**Lord Aldric Caldren** (human, 61) — the disputed House head. Not evil — desperate. Has convinced himself that developing the Isle is legitimate and that the Galapa can be accommodated somehow. Is not looking closely at how his subordinates are pursuing the claim.

**Lady Petra Caldren** (human, 34) — Aldric's niece, the rival claimant to the House leadership. She is the one actually running the Isle operation and she is considerably less concerned about the Galapa than her uncle. Has made quiet contact with House Vael about potential partnership — the Vaels' resources plus Caldren's maritime claim could be mutually beneficial.

## Campaign Arc Structure

# ACT ONE — The Fracture
## Sessions 1-28 (approximately)

*Lumencrest is cracking. The crisis has been building for thirty years. The players arrive just as the pressure finds its first release.*

---

### The Opening — Session 1-2
**Setting:** Lumencrest, Lamplight Ward and surroundings

Players arrive in Lumencrest through whatever circumstances their backstories establish. They may know each other or be strangers. The city hits them in the way the city hits everyone: the lights, the scale, the hum, the cable cars, the overwhelming sense of a civilization operating at a level nothing else matches.

Within their first day they become aware of the protests. The rationing of power to certain districts — including one that has never experienced rationing before — has produced organized demonstrations in Lamplight Ward and at the border of Shadowside. The protests are loud but not violent. City watch is present but not aggressive. The tension is present but manageable.

**Session 1 or 2 — The Explosion:**

The players are in or near a Lumen Network distribution hub — perhaps following a job lead, perhaps just passing through, perhaps someone they've met is in the area. The protest nearby is the largest in weeks. And then the hub explodes.

Not a massive explosion — targeted, precise, the work of someone who knew what they were doing. The distribution node goes down. A section of Lamplight Ward loses power simultaneously. In the chaos of the explosion and the sudden darkness, people are hurt. Some seriously. The protest crowd panics. City watch responds in force.

The players are in the middle of it.

**What happens next depends on the players:**
- Do they help the injured?
- Do they pursue whoever did this?
- Do they get caught up in the watch response and have to explain their presence?
- Do they notice things in the chaos that others miss?

This is not a combat encounter (necessarily). It is a situation that immediately establishes: this city is not stable, something is happening, and the players are now part of it.

**GM notes:** Wren and Ash are not present at the explosion. They are watching from a distance. Ash is troubled by the injuries. Wren is calculating whether the operation achieved its objective.

---

### Act One — The Shape of It (Sessions 3-28)

Act One is not a linear series of missions. It is a city the players are learning to navigate while the city itself becomes increasingly unstable. The following are the major beats — they can happen in different orders depending on player choices.

**Beat 1: The Investigation**
Someone hires the players — or they decide independently — to find out who bombed the hub. This pulls them into Lumencrest's political ecosystem. City watch has their own investigation (going nowhere useful). The Council factions each have theories that conveniently blame their rivals. The players are the only ones actually following the evidence.

The trail leads, eventually, to the Green Covenant radical wing. But getting there takes time, involves navigating multiple factions, and requires the players to decide: do they report what they find to the Council, or do they handle it another way? Both choices have consequences.

**Beat 2: The Factions Reveal Themselves**
As players move through the city, the major factions begin approaching them. The Ironwright Collective wants people who can carry messages that city watch might intercept. The Illuminists want discreet assistance with something in the Academic Quarter they can't use official channels for. The Conservators want someone to look into what the Expansionists are doing outside Council sessions. The Silver Thread offers information at a price. The Free Hand is watching the players to see if they're trustworthy.

Players begin building faction reputation organically — not by choosing allegiances explicitly but by whose jobs they take and how they handle them.

**Beat 3: The Second Brownout (and Third)**
Power failures continue. Each one is worse than the last. The Council's inability to respond effectively becomes increasingly public. Sevan Vael gives a speech in the Accord Chamber calling for calm and measured response that is widely praised and accomplishes nothing. Players who are paying attention may notice that Vael's estate has never experienced a brownout and that certain Council members seem less surprised by the failures than they should be.

**Beat 4: Shadowside Disappearances**
Someone in Shadowside — a regular at the Dark Lantern, a contact the players have made, a refugee they helped — mentions that people are going missing. Not many. Carefully. The kind of disappearances that don't get reported because the people doing the reporting are the same kind of people as the ones disappearing.

This is the first thread that leads toward the Unseen Guild's shard experiments. Players can pull on it now or file it away. The thread doesn't go away.

**Beat 5: The Refugees Arrive**
The first significant wave of Frostwell refugees reaches Lumencrest — not in large numbers, but enough to be visible. They cluster near Shadowside and Copper Gate. They bring stories: the tundra is wrong, the sky at night is different, things in the far north feel like they're ending. Most Lumencrest residents treat this as distant noise. Players from certain backgrounds may treat it as something more personal.

This is the first organic tug toward the Frostwell. It is not a mission. It is the world telling the players that something is happening elsewhere.

**Beat 6: The Radical Wing Identified**
Players who pursued the investigation reach a point where they know who bombed the hub — the Green Covenant radical wing, specifically Wren's cell. What they do with this information is a genuine choice:

- Report to the Council (Wren is captured or flees, the radical wing is disrupted but martyred, the Covenant mainstream faces political backlash)
- Confront Wren directly (possible to negotiate, possible to convince Ash to turn, possible to create an arrangement)
- Use the information as leverage with other factions
- Sit on it and watch what happens

There is no obviously correct answer. Each choice reshapes Act One's final beats.

**Beat 7: The Expansionist Move**
Players with sufficient faction reputation and Radiant Core access begin to hear rumors: the Expansionists are not just arguing for military preparation in the Council chamber. They are actively preparing. Steelwatch's Marshal-General has been in quiet correspondence. There are people moving through Lumencrest who look military without wearing military insignia.

And there is something else — a rumor, unconfirmed, that the Expansionists know something about the Hollostar that they haven't shared with the rest of the Council. Something that makes them more certain than they should be that seizing the Endless Eye's star is the right move.

**Beat 8: House Vael's First Touch**
At some point in Act One — the timing depends on player choices and faction reputation — Sevan Vael becomes aware of the players. Not as targets. As potentially useful people. He is too careful to approach them directly early. Instead: an invitation to a Council social event. A job offer that comes through three intermediaries. A favor extended at an unexpected moment.

Players may not realize who Vael is for several sessions. That's fine. The goal is to establish him as a presence before he becomes a known antagonist.

**Act One Climax — Sessions 25-28:**

The radical wing attempts a second, larger attack. This one targets multiple hubs simultaneously. If successful, significant portions of Lumencrest go dark for an extended period — long enough that the Council is forced to make public statements, long enough that the Manufactoories' machinery stops, long enough that the water pumping system begins to strain.

Whether this second attack succeeds depends on what the players have done in the preceding sessions. If they identified and disrupted the radical wing earlier, the attack is smaller or fails. If they sat on the information, it succeeds with full consequences.

Either way, Act One ends with:
- Lumencrest's political situation measurably worse
- The players with established faction relationships and reputations
- At least one major secret known to them that they haven't fully acted on
- A reason — personal, professional, or driven by something they've discovered — to look beyond Lumencrest

**The tug outward:** Something pulls them toward the world. A job. A lead. A refugee's story. A piece of information from a dying radical wing member. A message from someone they've never met in a place they've never been. The expedition begins.

# ACT TWO — THE EXPEDITION
## Sessions 29-70 (approximately)

*The world is larger than Lumencrest. The answers the players need are not in any single place. They are scattered across the continent in fragments — in sealed archives, in the memory of an 800-year-old elf, in a Frostvault mechanism that ran for twenty minutes three years ago and hasn't run since, in the words of a Dragon Turtle that nobody knows is a Dragon Turtle.*

*Act Two is about going looking.*

---

### The Shape of Act Two

Act Two is the most open-ended arc. Players have leads — multiple of them — and the order in which they pursue them is largely their choice. The GM's job is to make each location feel alive and consequential, to drop fragments of the larger truth in each place, and to ensure that the villain ecosystem is active and developing whether the players are watching it or not.

**What the players are building toward:** A complete enough picture of what Hollowstars actually are that they can make an informed decision about what to do in Act Three.

**What is happening while they travel:** House Vael is consolidating. Lyra is deepening the shard experiments. The Expansionists are getting closer to a Council vote on military action against the Endless Eye. The Dragon Turtle is continuing to wake up. The Frostwell evacuation is worsening. House Caldren is filing legal claims on the Isle.

The world does not pause for the expedition. The players are racing a clock they can't fully see yet.

---

### The Expedition Locations

These can be visited in any order. Each has its own story, its own local conflict, and its own fragment of the larger truth.

---

**Location 1: The Northern Expanse and Frostwell Border**

*The human cost, made personal*

The players travel north. The refugee crisis is real and visible — Celebrantford overwhelmed, Ironmere's gate closed, King Elowen unable to make a decision, Elder Valandil preparing his secret caverns for a catastrophe he hasn't told anyone about.

**What they find:** People making impossible choices. The Nine Hearths communities split between those who stayed and those who fled. Families divided. The specific human face of what it means for a Hollostar to be falling.

**The truth fragment:** Elder Valandil — if players find him and earn enough trust — has seen a Hollostar fall before. He was young. He describes what he saw: not destruction, but change. A region transformed over years. He doesn't know what the star is. He knows the world was different after, and the difference was not entirely bad. He has been preparing not because he thinks everyone will die, but because he thinks the immediate impact will be devastating even if the long-term is something else.

**The Unseen Guild thread:** If players are following the Shadowside disappearance thread, some of those disappearances connect to refugees in transit through this region. The Collector has been operating here. There may be evidence, if players look.

**The local conflict:** King Elowen's paralysis has produced a vacuum. Commander Yara Stonefield wants to close the passes. Advisor Theron Mael wants open borders. Captain Brek Coldmarch is the right answer and doesn't want the job. Players who engage with this might help resolve the Warden vacancy — which changes how the evacuation unfolds in Act Three.

---

**Location 2: The Glittering Marshes**

*What the shards do to living things — and what the living things do to the shards*

The Glittering Marshes cannot be entered without Ribbet guides. They will not guide players to the shard cluster sites without reason to trust them. Earning that trust takes time and requires demonstrating respect for the marsh's culture and ecology.

**What they find:** Deepsilt, built over the largest shard cluster on the continent. Deep Bloom, the fungal colony that has been incorporating shard fragments into its biological structure. Something in the deep marsh that the Ribbet won't name directly but circle around in conversation — a presence in the shards themselves, they say. Not a voice. Not a person. Something like memory.

**The truth fragment:** The fungal colony in Deep Bloom has been *learning* from the shards. Not in a way that produces speech or clear communication, but in a way that Fungril elders can interface with through their mycelium connection. What they receive is not language — it's impression. Feeling. The overwhelmingly consistent impression is: *longing*. Something in the shards misses something it has been away from for a very long time.

Players who spend real time in Deep Bloom and find a way to interface with this (through a Fungril elder, through a player character with relevant abilities) may receive a fragmentary impression: an image of a civilization, people who look nothing like any ancestry on the continent, and a sky full of stars, and then a choice, and then darkness, and then a long falling.

This is not conclusive. It is a piece. Players who understand what they're seeing will recognize it in Act Three when the full picture assembles.

**The local conflict:** The Somnarchs and Starfall Seekers both want access to the shard clusters the Ribbet control. The Ribbet are saying no. Someone — the players may eventually realize it's the corrupted Seeker researchers connected to the Unseen Guild — has been trying to find alternative access routes into the deep marsh that bypass the Ribbet guide requirement. The Ribbet know someone has been trying. They don't know who.

---

**Location 3: The Verdant Crescent — Deep Forest and Heartgrove**

*The records that should not be sealed*

The Green Covenant's Archcovenant Maren Stillwater has 2,000-year records of two previous Hollostar landings. She is the only person who has read them in their entirety. She has not decided what to do with what she knows.

Getting access to these records is not simple. The Heartgrove is the Green Covenant's sacred center. Access requires either the Archcovenant's personal permission or a way to reach the records without her knowing — both of which require earning significant trust with the mainstream Covenant, which is complicated if players were involved in the radical wing situation in Act One.

**What they find:** The records describe two previous landings in detail — not mechanically, but in the way that people describe something they witnessed without understanding it. The land changed. There was destruction and then there was transformation. The records include a phrase that appears in both accounts, in the language of the civilization that preceded the Covenant's founding: a phrase that the Covenant has never been able to translate.

Players who have also been to the Glittering Marshes and received the Deep Bloom impression may suddenly understand what the phrase means. It is a name. The name of what the Hollowstars are in their own language. It means, approximately: *the ones who return to give*.

**The local conflict:** The radical wing situation from Act One has consequences here. If players reported or disrupted the radical wing, the mainstream Covenant is grateful but also politically exposed — their own members did this, and there are factions within the Covenant debating how to respond. Thornwall, the radical wing's Heartgrove contact, is here and does not know whether players identified them. This is a tension the players can choose to resolve or leave unresolved.

The Lumencrest timber pressure is ongoing. The Covenant needs allies who can negotiate with the Council. Players with sufficient Lumencrest faction reputation may be in a position to broker something — or to promise something they'll need to deliver on later.

---

**Location 4: The Isle of the Broken Sphere**

*The island that isn't an island*

Players arrive on the Isle — possibly following the Silverbelt political thread, possibly drawn by rumors of unusual geological activity, possibly because Elder Tess sent word through the Galapa trade network to someone they know.

**What they find:** A settlement (Shellhaven) that is carrying on with deliberate normalcy while the ground occasionally shakes and the interior forest dies back toward the coast. The Galapa community, led by Elder Tess. Three interior settlements recently relocated to Shellhaven without public announcement. The Spine Stones rising through the soil. The warmth in the fissures. The heartbeat tremors.

**The truth fragment:** Elder Tess has been meditating at the Spine Stones for decades. She has reached *something*. She cannot describe it in words exactly — vastness, she says, confusion, curiosity, something that has been asleep for so long it doesn't yet know where it is. She has felt it become more aware in recent months. She believes it knows the Galapa are here. She believes it is trying to understand them.

Players who work with Elder Tess on this — who find a way to deepen or extend the contact — may receive communication: not words, but sensation. Size without limit. Time measured in centuries. And a direction — a pull toward somewhere. The Dragon Turtle is waking because the Hollostar's approach is affecting every ancient thing on the continent. It is responding to something it knows on a level older than thought.

Players may or may not piece together what the Isle actually is during this visit. They will definitely know something is very wrong, very old, and very alive beneath them.

**The House Caldren conflict:** House Caldren's survey teams have been to the Isle. They are filing legal claims. Lady Petra Caldren may even be present or arrive during the players' visit. The conflict between Caldren's claim and the Galapa community's right to exist on the Isle is a concrete political problem the players can engage with — and resolving it (or failing to) has direct consequences for what happens in Act Three when the Dragon Turtle actually moves.

**The House Vael connection:** Lady Petra has made contact with Lyra Vael about partnership. If players have been tracking House Vael, they may find evidence of this connection here — a letter, a meeting, a financial arrangement in the survey team's records.

---

**Location 5: The Frostwell Tundra — Frostvault**

*The answers, finally*

This is the expedition's climax. Players reach Frostvault — the pre-civilization buried complex that the Starfall Seekers have been excavating for decades. Getting here requires navigating the Frostwell crisis, the refugee movement, and the increasing strangeness of the northern landscape as the approaching Hollostar's energy begins affecting the region.

**What they find:** Frostvault's six explored levels, the Starfall Seeker permanent outpost, and access to the seventh level — deeper than any excavation has gone, accessible now because the approach of the Hollostar has done something to the mechanisms that sealed it. The lock has opened. Not because anyone unlocked it. Because whoever locked it designed it to open when the right conditions were met. And those conditions are: a Hollostar is close enough to resonate with the mechanisms inside.

**The seventh level:** A chamber unlike anything above it. Not a research space or residential area — a recording space. The walls are covered in the same symbols found at the Shattered Spire, in Frostvault's upper levels, on the Sleeping Gate. But here they are organized differently — not random symbols but a continuous document. And in the center of the chamber is a mechanism. Not the mechanism from the third level that ran for twenty minutes three years ago. A different one. Larger. And it is active — a low hum, the same frequency as the Lumen Network but different, resonating with something in the approaching Hollostar.

**The truth revealed:** With sufficient scholarship, magical assistance, or simply enough time, the players can understand what the chamber records. It is not a history. It is a message — written to whoever came after, to whoever found this place when the stars returned. It describes, in language that can be parsed with enough effort, what the Hollowstars are. Where they went. Why they went. What they are doing when they fall. And what the civilization that wrote this was hoping to find when they came back.

They were hoping to find their children. They believed that life, given enough time and a few gifts of energy and water and warmth, would eventually produce people who carried something forward — not a genetic line, not a specific culture, but something of what they were. The capacity for community. For sacrifice. For choice.

They don't know if it worked. They couldn't know, from inside a star. But they had to try.

**The Sleeping Gate:** Near the projected impact zone. Players who visit it before the landing discover the symbols are changing faster now — almost daily. The Gate is responding to the approaching Hollostar. It is not a gate in the sense of a door. It is a receiver. Whatever the Hollostar carries, the Gate is designed to receive it and release it gradually rather than all at once — a way of making the gift survivable for the world it lands in.

**This changes the calculus entirely:** Redirecting the Hollostar away from the Sleeping Gate means the gift, whatever it is, releases without the Gate's mediation. Which means much more destruction before the transformation. The Frostwell impact with the Gate in place: significant but survivable, with transformation over years. Without the Gate: catastrophic impact followed eventually by the same transformation but at far greater immediate cost.

The players now have enough information to make real decisions. Act Three begins.

---

### Act Two — The Villain Threads Running Parallel

While the players expedition, the following is happening without them:

**House Vael:** Sevan has positioned himself as the Council's most trusted moderate voice. He has been quietly ensuring that the Illuminists' research programs produce nothing actionable — not through sabotage, but through steering the Board of Research toward approving programs that seem promising but won't produce results in time. Lyra has expanded the shard experiment operation. Three new subjects have survived transformation in ways that are being carefully evaluated.

**The Unseen Guild:** Mira Voss has identified that the players are investigating the Shadowside disappearances. She has not moved against them — they're not important enough yet, and drawing attention is bad for the operation. But the Collector has been warned and the disappearances have temporarily paused in Lumencrest. They have not paused in the Northern Expanse.

**The Expansionists:** A Council vote on military authorization has been tabled twice. The third attempt is being prepared. Theron Ashwick has secured Sevan Vael's conditional support — Vael will vote for authorization if specific conditions are attached that appear to limit scope but are written in a way that doesn't actually limit scope. This is moving forward.

**House Caldren:** The Isle legal claim is filed. The Galapa are being notified that their "transient occupancy" is under legal review. Elder Tess is buying time through procedural means she can't sustain indefinitely.

**The Frostwell:** More hearths are evacuating, or trying to. The frontier towns of the Northern Expanse are at or beyond capacity. King Elowen still hasn't appointed a Warden. The Dragon Turtle's tremors are becoming a news item in the coastal cities — dismissed by scholars as geological activity but noticed by sailors who work those waters.

---

# ACT THREE — THE LANDING AND WHAT COMES AFTER
## Sessions 71-130 (approximately)

*The players know what the Hollowstars are. The Hollostar is close enough now that everyone can see it — a new star in the northern sky, brighter every week. The window for decisions is closing. Everything the players have built across two acts — relationships, reputation, knowledge, faction standing — is now the currency they spend on the choices that matter.*

---

### The Opening of Act Three — The Convergence

Players return to Lumencrest (or converge there from wherever they ended Act Two) to find the city transformed by what has happened in their absence:

- The Council has voted (or is about to vote) on military authorization for action against the Endless Eye
- The Hollostar is visible in the night sky and has been for weeks — a bright point of light that moves differently than stars should
- Refugees from the Frostwell are now arriving in Lumencrest in numbers the city cannot ignore
- Sevan Vael has given three major public speeches and is widely considered the most steady voice in the Council
- Something has happened with the Shadowside disappearances — they've resumed, and this time one of the missing is someone with a name enough people know that it can't be ignored

The players arrive knowing things the city doesn't. The question is what they do with that knowledge.

---

### The Central Decisions of Act Three

These are not sequential missions. They are pressures that build simultaneously and eventually force resolution.

**Decision 1: The Military Vote**
The Expansionists are pushing for authorization to redirect the Hollostar to the Endless Eye — essentially, to seize the star's energy for Lumencrest by destroying the city that currently sits above it and replacing the failing Heart. Players who understand what the Hollostar actually is know this is not just politically wrong — it is a specific kind of violation. They also know the Endless Eye's existing star is failing, so the plan may not even work.

Stopping the vote requires: sufficient faction reputation with Conservators and/or Illuminists to influence Council members, evidence of what Ashwick has been planning outside official channels, and potentially exposing Sevan Vael's role in enabling the vote — which means exposing House Vael, which means Lyra's operation becomes public, which means everything House Vael has been building collapses simultaneously.

This is only possible if players have built sufficient Lumencrest faction reputation. If they haven't, the vote passes and Act Three must accommodate a world where Lumencrest is attempting military action against the Endless Eye while simultaneously managing a falling Hollostar in the north.

**Decision 2: The Hollostar Redirection Question**
Players now know the redirection mechanism exists (in or connected to Frostvault and the Sleeping Gate). They know what each option means:

- **Let it land in Frostwell as intended** — the Sleeping Gate mediates the impact, significant but survivable, Frostwell transforms over years. The human cost is whatever remains in the impact zone.
- **Redirect to open sea** — no mediation, more destructive impact, but no civilization directly destroyed. Over years an island forms — fertile, resource-rich, immediately politically contested. The gift is spent on water and nobody asked for.
- **Redirect to Lumencrest** — two Hollowstars interacting. Nobody knows what happens. The records in Frostvault's seventh level don't address this scenario. It might save the city. It might destroy it. It might produce something unprecedented.
- **Don't redirect — evacuate completely** — the players use everything they have to get every remaining person out of the impact zone. This requires solving the Northern Expanse political crisis, getting King Elowen to act, convincing the staying Nine Hearths communities, and coordinating the Eagle Riders. It is logistically the hardest option. It may not be fully achievable.

**Decision 3: House Vael**
The shard experiments are producing results. Lyra has three subjects who survived transformation with useful capabilities. She is moving toward the next phase — wider testing, possible application. If players expose the operation, they save the current and future victims but also collapse House Vael's political position at a critical moment — and Sevan Vael is currently one of the few people with enough Council credibility to potentially moderate the Expansionist military vote. Exposing him may stop the shard experiments but accelerate the military action.

There is no clean answer. That is intentional.

**Decision 4: The Dragon Turtle**
The Isle is moving. House Caldren's claim is active. The Galapa need either legal protection or evacuation. And the Dragon Turtle is going *somewhere* — the direction of travel is becoming apparent. Depending on where it goes, the political implications vary dramatically. If it moves toward the Silverbelt Coast, it creates a new landmass adjacent to the Four Houses' territory. If it moves toward open water, it eventually settles somewhere new.

Players who built a relationship with Elder Tess may be able to influence the Dragon Turtle's direction — not control it, but nudge it. The creature is intelligent. It responded to Elder Tess's attention. It might respond to a deliberate, sincere communication from people who understand what it is.

### The Climax

The Hollostar's arrival is not a single scene. It is a sequence:

**The week before:** The Hollostar is close enough to affect the Lumen Network. The Heart of Lumencrest — which has been failing for thirty years — begins to stabilize. The Illuminists can't explain why. Petra Vane can. She has been trying to tell them for months. The Heart isn't failing. It's responding. It responds to the approaching Hollostar because they are the same kind of thing. Two parts of the same civilization, one buried and one arriving.

**The night before:** The Sleeping Gate activates. Every symbol on it illuminates simultaneously. In Frostvault, the central chamber mechanism begins running — not for twenty minutes this time. Continuously. The Starfall Seeker outpost sends urgent messages to every major city. The message is six words: *Something in the Gate is awake.*

**The landing:** Depends entirely on what the players have chosen. Every option produces a different event. Let it land in Frostwell with the Gate: a light, a sound felt through the ground, and then warmth spreading outward over days. Without the Gate: impact, destruction, and then warmth spreading outward over years. Redirected to sea: a column of water visible from three hundred miles. Redirected to Lumencrest: something nobody planned for.

**The aftermath:** The Frostwell — or wherever it landed — begins changing. The change is slow enough that players can see its beginning but not its end. Refugees in the Northern Expanse begin asking whether it's safe to go back. The political situation in Lumencrest shifts based on all the decisions made in Act Three. The Dragon Turtle is somewhere new. House Vael is either collapsed or restructured depending on whether players exposed them.

---

### The Ending — What Comes From Vossomar

As things settle — not all at once, but over the last few sessions of Act Three — something arrives.

It is not a fleet. It is not an army. It is a single ship, unlike any ship design on the continent, arriving at Montauseau's harbor from the west. The ship carries people who look recognizably human but are not quite right in ways that take time to identify. They speak a language nobody recognizes, though some words are close to old Rouçon trading dialect in ways that are almost certainly not coincidental.

They are not hostile. They are not friendly. They are *assessing*.

Trade Envoy Thessaly Renne, who has been watching Vossomar for signs of what was coming, is the first official to meet them. She sends a message to Empress Carmine that reads, in its entirety: *It has begun. What I don't know yet is whether it's the beginning of something good.*

The campaign ends there. The players know what it means but don't know what happens next. That is the next campaign — a different time, a changed continent, the world they made.

## Cross-Arc Tracking — Things That Continue Regardless of Session

**Faction Reputation:** Players accumulate reputation with all Lumencrest factions, regional factions, and world factions continuously. The political options available in Act Three are directly determined by what they built in Acts One and Two.

**The Shard Experiment Thread:** Lyra's operation continues in the background throughout Acts One and Two. Each time players touch it and don't fully resolve it, it grows. The number of subjects, the quality of results, the scope of operations — all expand on a timeline independent of player attention.

**The Heart's Decline (and stabilization):** Power output data from the Lumen Network is a visible real-world metric. Players can ask about it. It declines predictably until the Hollostar gets close enough in Act Three that it begins to stabilize — which is the first physical confirmation that what Petra Vane suspected is true.

**The Sleeping Gate's symbols:** Changing throughout the campaign. Players who visit early and late see different configurations. The rate of change accelerates as the Hollostar approaches.

**Cindrath:** The dragon in the Furnace volcano continues granting audiences and twisting wishes throughout the campaign. One wish — granted to an Expansionist operative seeking military intelligence on the Endless Eye sometime in Act Two — begins to twist in Act Three in a way that could destabilize the volcanic region of the Desert of Glass. Players who are tracking this can intervene. Players who aren't may have a late Act Three complication they didn't see coming.

## The Questions the Campaign Asks

These are the questions that should be alive in every session, even when the players are doing something apparently unrelated:

1. What do you owe the people you come from?
2. Is a gift still a gift if the recipient can't consent to receiving it?
3. Who gets to decide what a civilization becomes?
4. What is the difference between a sacrifice and a crime?
5. If someone did terrible things for reasons that turn out to be based on a misunderstanding — are they still responsible for the terrible things?
