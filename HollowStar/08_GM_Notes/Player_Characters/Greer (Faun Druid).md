# FAUN DRUID — CHARACTER KNOWLEDGE BRIEFING

### What Your Character Knows After Sixty Years of Living, Watching, and Listening

---

_This document covers what your character knows going into the campaign. You grew up in Rivermark Sovereignty, lost your home forest to logging, moved with your family to the Ember Grove in the Central Heartlands, and have spent the last twenty-five years there as a practicing druid with Green Covenant connections. Sixty years is long enough to understand how the world works. Use it._

# THE PATH OF SPIRITS

## Your Faith — What You Know, What You Practice, What You Feel

---

### The Core of It

- Everything has spirit. This is not metaphor or poetry. It is the foundational truth of your faith and your direct experience confirms it. Trees, rivers, stones, storms, animals — all alive, all aware, all connected through an invisible web that a trained practitioner can feel and, with time and respect, communicate with.
- The world is divided into three realms: the Mortal Realm (where we live), the Spirit Realm (separate, timeless, genuinely dangerous), and the Threshold (thin places where the two touch — sacred groves, ancient trees, certain springs, certain stones).
- Spirits exist in a hierarchy of scale, not of worth. Small spirits are everywhere — individual trees, household spirits, the spirit of a particular bend in a river. Great Spirits are the named ones — The Forest, The Mountain, The River, the ancient animal spirits. Ancient Spirits are rarely contacted and almost never sought directly. Most practitioners never speak with one. Those who do are changed by it.

---

### What You Do Every Day

- You ask permission before cutting any plant, even grass. You thank water before drinking. You acknowledge the spirit of any animal you eat. These are not ceremonies — they are habits as automatic as breathing.
- Offerings before significant actions. Not large. Not expensive. The spirit of the act matters more than the material value. A handful of seeds before entering a forest. A drop of water returned to its source. A moment of genuine acknowledgment before using a thing.
- You carry a drum. You use it for ritual, for spirit communion, for focus. The heartbeat rhythm connects to the earth's pulse. You have used it since childhood. It is the most reliable tool you own.
- You read the world constantly. Birds are the easiest signal — their behavior tells you things before you have reason to know them. Weather patterns, animal movements, the specific quality of silence in a forest before something changes. Sixty years of this makes it instinct.

---

### Ancestor Spirits and Death

- When a person dies, they become a spirit. The quality of that spirit depends on how they lived. Shamans and those marked by the Path typically become greater spirits. Ordinary people become lesser ones.
- Your ancestors watch over your family. This is not comforting mythology. You have received guidance through dreams that you cannot attribute to your own mind. Your grandmother visited you twice after she died. The first time you were frightened. The second time you were grateful.
- The Spirit Realm is not peaceful rest. It is an active, dangerous, ongoing trial. Spirits who navigate it well earn the right to return. Most who reincarnate remember nothing of previous lives. Those who remember are always Path of Spirits followers — which your tradition takes as direct evidence of its truth.

---

### Hollowstars — What the Path Believes

- Hollowstars are another form of spirit energy. They are not gods. They are not to be worshiped. They are forces — like storms, like rivers — powerful and dangerous and useful if approached with respect.
- They have spirits of their own. These spirits are vast and strange and very difficult to communicate with. Some shamans have tried. Most fail. Those who succeed describe the experience as speaking with something so large it cannot perceive you the way you perceive a person.
- Using Hollostar power is permitted within the Path, provided it is done with proper ritual and genuine respect. Some practitioners refuse entirely — they find the energy "too loud, too bright." This is a personal decision and both approaches are respected.
- The transformed creatures that appear near Hollostar sites are, in the Path's understanding, spirits trying to communicate through mutation. They are messengers, not monsters. They are also genuinely dangerous. The appropriate response is to study them, understand the message, and then kill them mercifully — they suffer, and releasing them is an act of care.

---

### What the Spirits Are Telling You Right Now

- Over the last decade — gradually enough that you almost missed it — the spiritual presences in the Central Heartlands have become quieter. Not absent. Quieter. The small spirits still respond. The Great Spirits are still there. But there is a quality of listening, of held breath, as if something large is paying attention and everything smaller has stopped talking so as not to interrupt it.
- You noticed this first in the Ember Grove, around eight years ago. You thought initially it was something local — a problem with the Grove, or your own perception. Then you spoke with druids from other communities passing through Emberford, and they described something similar, in different language, about their own places.
- You have not told Thorn Oakwise this in those exact terms. You have told him the spirits seem watchful. He nodded as if he already knew.
- This is not a crisis, as far as you can determine. It is a signal. You do not yet know what it signals toward.

---

### Practitioners — The Difference Between Druids and Shamans

- Both are Path of Spirits practitioners. The distinction is one of focus and role.
- **Shamans** are community-facing. They mediate between their people and the spirit world, perform ceremonies, interpret spirit communications for others, handle the dead. They are anchored to a community.
- **Druids** are grove-anchored. Their primary relationship is with the spirit of a specific place — a sacred forest, a grove, a particular landscape. They protect that place, maintain its spiritual health, and act as its voice. They may serve a broader community but their first obligation is to the land itself.
- You are a druid. The Ember Grove is your place. But you grew up in a community with strong shaman traditions in Rivermark, and your practice has elements of both. This is not unusual for someone who moved as a child.
- Druidic circles are networked but decentralized. There is no hierarchy among druids the way there might be in a structured religious institution. What there is instead is a web of relationships, obligations, and shared knowledge. You know druids in three different communities well enough to ask them for help. They know you the same way.

---

### Sacred Practices Worth Knowing

- **Spirit Communion** — Direct communication with spirits. Requires focus, often the drum, sometimes specific herbs. Easier in places with strong spiritual presence. Harder in cities where the spirit web is thin and confused.
- **Reading the Land** — Sixty years of practice means you can feel the spiritual health of a landscape the way another person might feel the temperature of a room. Not precise enough to be a detailed map, but accurate enough to notice wrongness.
- **The Spirit Journey** — Entering a trance state and traveling into the Spirit Realm to seek information or assistance. Genuinely dangerous. Not something done casually. You have done this four times. All four times you came back changed in small ways.
- **Offerings and Reciprocity** — The economic logic of the spirit world. Give, and the relationship builds. Take without giving, and the relationship degrades. Apply this to everything.

# THE GREEN COVENANT

## The Organization You Know, But Are Not Yet Part Of

---

### What They Are

- The oldest and most powerful nature-protection organization on the continent. Not a government, not an army, not a religion — something harder to define and harder to oppose. Based in the Verdant Crescent's deep forest at a sacred site called the Heartgrove.
- All Green Covenant members follow the Path of Spirits. This is non-negotiable and enforced. But most Path of Spirits practitioners are not Green Covenant. Being a druid does not make you Covenant. The Covenant is roughly 400-500 active members continent-wide. Small, precise, and deeply networked.
- Their oath: _"I place the balance before myself, before my kind, before my preferences. The forest endures. I am its voice for as long as I am worthy."_

---

### How They Are Structured

- **The Archcovenant** leads. Currently **Maren Stillwater**, a human woman of 67, only the third human to hold the title. She was selected twelve years ago through the forest's direct choice — she spent time alone in the Heartgrove and a living vine appeared around her left forearm. This is how every Archcovenant is selected. Nobody is told it will happen. Nobody can engineer it.
- **Five Wardens** manage regional responsibilities below the Archcovenant. The ones you know about:
    - **Warden of the Heartgrove** — Elder Fenn Mossgrow, 154-year-old Simiah. Protects the sacred center.
    - **Warden of the Edge** — Thalia Greenmark, 44. Manages relations with border settlements, handles timber negotiations.
    - **Warden of Waters** — Kael Streamrunner, a Ribbet. Manages rivers and wetlands.
    - **Warden of Memory** — Seren Longbranch, 203-year-old Simiah. Maintains historical archives.
    - **Warden of the Reach** — Daven Ashwood, half-elf. Manages Covenant agents outside the Verdant Crescent.
- Below the Wardens: Grove Keepers (20-30 active), Rangers (around 200), Initiates (around 100).

---

### How They Actually Operate

- First response is always negotiation. Formal protest, legal challenge, community organizing, public pressure.
- When negotiation fails, things begin to happen that nobody claims responsibility for. Logging equipment fails repeatedly. Supply chains are disrupted. Camps burn. The Covenant never acts publicly, never claims responsibility, leaves no documented connection to leadership.
- Lumencrest knows. They cannot prove it.
- This is the part you have complicated feelings about. You understand the logic. You are not certain the logic always leads somewhere you can follow.

---

### The Internal Divide — Moderates and the Thorns

- Roughly 85% of the Covenant follows the moderate position: work within systems, escalate only when necessary, maintain sustainable relationships with settlements, protect the forest by making the forest something the world wants to protect.
- The Thorns are the remaining 15%, operating as a secret faction within the Covenant. They believe negotiation has failed. They want all logging near ancient groves ended — not reduced, ended. They have been connected to the three burned logging camps from a decade ago, recurring equipment failures at Lumencrest-connected operations, and the disappearance of two Lumencrest surveyors four years ago who were mapping deep forest approaches.
- The Thorns are not officially acknowledged by the Covenant. The Covenant does not discuss their existence in formal communications.

---

### Where Your Family Stands

- Your parents are Covenant-adjacent. Their sympathies run closer to the Thorns than they have ever said directly to you. You believe this based on what they say and more on what they do not say. You have not asked them directly because you are not ready for the answer to change how you see them. This is a decision you know you will have to revisit.
- You understand the Thorns. Goldwood was logged out from under your family while agreements were honored in letter and violated in spirit, while the money offered kept going up and the protections kept getting quietly renegotiated. The anger that makes someone join the Thorns is not something you have to imagine. You carry it.
- You do not believe violence — or actions that could get people killed — is the right path. Not because the forest is worth less. Because burning things down rarely controls what catches fire next, and the Covenant's power has always been that it is harder to dismiss than an army.

---

### Thorn Oakwise and the Ember Grove

- **Thorn Oakwise**, human, mid-fifties, has lived in the Ember Grove year-round for decades. He is not a warm man in a social sense. He is a present man — fully there in every conversation, which can feel like warmth if you are used to people being elsewhere in their heads.
- He accepted your family into the Grove after a period of observation. He began your formal druidic training after another period of observation. He does not do things quickly but he does them with the full weight of his attention.
- The **Heart Stone** at the Grove's center — an ancient standing stone carved with symbols no scholar has successfully read — is a focal point for the Grove's spiritual presence. It has been warm to the touch for two years. Thorn knows. He has not said what he thinks it means.
- The Grove's spiritual presence is real and responsive. Animals behave differently here. Compasses spin. Some visitors report feeling watched. Trees appear to move when unobserved. All of this is, from your experience, accurate.
- Thorn sent you to Lumencrest. His stated reason: observe, understand, make contact with sympathetic parties. His actual concern, which he has not stated and you understand anyway: the timber offers being made to Emberford are getting larger, and the protection the Grove currently enjoys depends on a combination of law, local reverence, and Covenant awareness. One of those three is easier to erode than the others.

# THE OTHER FAITHS

## What You Know and How You Understand Them

---

### The Six-Fold Faith

- The dominant religion of the Central Heartlands and the Northern Expanse. Six gods in three tiers — The Architect and The Weaver at the top (cosmic forces), The Sentinel and The Wanderer in the middle (active intermediaries), The Hearthkeeper and The Gravewalker closest to mortal concerns.
- The gods are in eternal tension with each other. This tension, in Six-Fold theology, is what creates existence. Followers pray not to change the gods but to align themselves with divine currents.
- **The Architect** (order, law, structure, civilization) is the primary deity of Lumencrest's technocratic class and Accord administration. His temple architecture is geometrically perfect and built to last centuries.
- **The Weaver** (change, wilderness, growth, chaos) is closer to the Path of Spirits in temperament than any other Six-Fold deity. Some of her followers drift toward the Path or hold both simultaneously. This is theologically contested in the Six-Fold tradition.
- **The Hearthkeeper** (home, family, community, belonging) is the most commonly worshiped in rural areas. The deity of ordinary daily life.
- **The Gravewalker** (death, transition, memory, the past) tends the dead. Path of Spirits practitioners sometimes feel a kinship with Gravewalker theology without sharing the underlying cosmology.
- Your position on the Six-Fold Faith: it is a genuine tradition with real practitioners and real spiritual experiences within it. You do not share its cosmology. You do not dismiss it. The Weaver in particular describes something real — the experience of the Six-Fold follower who communes with nature is having a real encounter, interpreted through a different framework than yours.

---

### The First Flame Faith

- A younger faith — about 300 years old — built on the "All-One Heresy": the claim that the Six Gods are not separate beings but six aspects of a single unified divine being called the Eternal Flame.
- They accept the Six-Fold cosmology but reinterpret it. The Architect is the Eternal Flame's aspect of order. The Weaver is its aspect of change. And so on.
- Growing quickly in the Central Heartlands, particularly among working-class urban populations. Their theology of divine light — a metaphor for truth, knowledge, and the divine presence within each person — has been resonating in communities where manufactured light is becoming unreliable.
- Led by **High Flame Kaida** and a council of Flamekeepers. Evangelical in approach but not aggressive about it. They genuinely believe they are sharing something important and their evangelism has that quality rather than a coercive one.
- Their relationship to the Path of Spirits: complicated. They consider Path practitioners to be spiritually aware but working with incomplete understanding — acknowledging real spirit presences but misidentifying their nature. Path practitioners tend to find the First Flame's single-deity framework too narrow to hold what is actually there.
- Your position: The First Flame is a living, growing faith with genuine spiritual experience at its core. Their tendency to frame Hollostar transformed creatures as "abominations" rather than messengers is where you part ways most sharply. Their cosmology is too tidy for the world as you have experienced it.

---

### The Forge-Father Faith

- The dominant faith of dwarven communities, including Ironhold. Not widely practiced outside dwarven culture but deeply embedded within it.
- Centers on the Forge-Father, a divine craftsman who shaped the world from raw chaos. Dwarven identity — labor, craft, material mastery, the depth of the earth — is understood as sacred participation in ongoing creation.
- Their relationship with the other faiths is respectful but distant. They do not proselytize. Their faith is theirs and they do not feel called to share it.
- Your contact with this tradition is limited. What you know comes from interactions with Drakona and dwarven travelers passing through Rivermark and Emberford. Enough to understand its broad shape and show appropriate respect.

# THE HEARTLAND ACCORD

## How It Works, What It Actually Is, and Where It Is Right Now

---

### The Structure

- Five city-states bound by treaty: **Lumencrest**, **Kingsrest**, **Ironhold**, **Steelwatch**, and **Hazelhaven**. Four hundred years old. The oldest political institution in the Central Heartlands.
- The founding principle was five equal partners cooperating voluntarily for shared benefit. In practice, Lumencrest's size and technological dominance meant it has always had more weight than the others, and the other cities have found it easier to work within that reality than fight it. That is changing.
- **The Accord Council** meets quarterly in Lumencrest. Lumencrest holds 12 seats by population formula, Kingsrest 7, Ironhold 6, Steelwatch 5. Smaller towns and Rivermark share 2 seats collectively. Rivermark's collective 2 seats are your people's voice in a body that makes decisions affecting the entire continent.

---

### What the Accord Can and Cannot Do

**Can:**

- Regulate inter-city trade and set tariffs
- Invoke mutual defense obligations
- Manage shared infrastructure (roads, bridges, river management)
- Arbitrate disputes between member cities
- Coordinate food reserves — currently the most contentious active issue

**Cannot:**

- Compel a city to act against its direct interests
- Override a city's internal governance
- Tax member cities directly — it runs on voluntary contributions
- Enforce decisions without consensus — which means when consensus fails, nothing is enforced

---

### The Five Cities — What You Know of Each

**Lumencrest**

- Dominant power. 80,000 people, more than any other city on the continent. Chairs the Accord Council by tradition and dominates it by weight.
- Internally paralyzed by three factions — Expansionists (aggressive growth, militarization), Conservators (stability, protect current structures), and Illuminists (reform, worker interests). They have not passed a significant measure in over a year.
- Lumencrest's internal deadlock is the Accord's deadlock. The other cities have noticed and are quietly reconsidering their positions.

**Kingsrest**

- Monarchy. Old noble families, hereditary rule, deeply conservative in a way that has served it for a long time and is now potentially dangerous. Agricultural heartland city.
- Actively opposes Rivermark's Sixth Seat campaign. Their objection is framed as concerns about "foreign influence" and "non-human majority." You know what this means in practice.
- King Aldric IV has ruled for 82 years. He is not incompetent. He is cautious in ways that no longer fit the speed the situation requires.

**Ironhold**

- Dwarven mountain city, 2,800 years of continuous habitation. Their perspective on the Accord is that it is a recent development, and recent developments are given proportionate weight. They respect competence above almost anything else.
- Their Clan Council structure makes them nearly impossible to manipulate from outside and nearly as difficult to move quickly from inside. Currently engaged in an internal debate about whether the Accord still serves their interests. The fact that this debate is ongoing without resolution is itself notable — Ironhold doesn't usually leave things unresolved.

**Steelwatch**

- Fortress city, built to garrison the Heartlands' northwestern approaches. Military governance. Has spent most of its existence being very good at a job that has never been necessary.
- Restless and frustrated by the Accord's inability to act on obvious crises. Marshal-General Corvain is the most vocal advocate in the Accord for a serious Frostwell response. She is running into the same wall of inaction as everyone else.
- The strongest Accord supporter of Rivermark's Sixth Seat campaign — they understand the value of the military buffer and the partnership.

**Hazelhaven**

- The Accord's quiet city. Trading focused, watching carefully, trying not to commit to any position before the dust settles. Currently undecided on the Sixth Seat vote.

---

### Where the Accord Is Right Now

- Fourteen months without a significant coordinated measure. Every major vote collapses into factional negotiation that serves no one.
- Food reserve coordination — theoretically the Accord's most essential current function — has become a source of open suspicion rather than cooperation. There are rumors that the reserve data being shared between cities does not match actual yields. If this surfaces publicly, it will threaten the Accord's legitimacy at exactly the moment the Accord needs to function.
- What holds it together: the cities genuinely need each other. Kingsrest's food, Ironhold's materials, Steelwatch's security, Lumencrest's technology — real interdependencies. The Accord doesn't need to be trusted to be useful. It just needs to be useful enough that breaking it costs more than maintaining it. That calculation has not yet tipped. But it is tipping.

# THE RIVERMARK SOVEREIGNTY

## Home — Where You Grew Up, What You Know From the Inside

---

### What Rivermark Is

- An elven-Faun kingdom 287 years old, east of the Ironspine Mountains, in the river valleys formed by the Crystalriver and the Goldwater, which converge at Twinwater into the Silverfork.
- Allied with the Accord for 135 years — not a member, an ally. The distinction is legally precise and politically significant. The alliance was built on mutual recognition that cooperation served both parties better than competition. Over 135 years it has deepened into something that feels increasingly arbitrary to separate.
- Population approximately 82,000. Primarily elven (55%), significant Faun presence (30%), Drakona in the southern mountains (10%), others (5%). Your people are not a minority here the way they are in the western Accord cities.

---

### The Confluence Council — How Rivermark Governs Itself

- The capital is **Twinwater**, built at the literal confluence of the two great rivers. The Confluence Council meets here.
- **12 River Voices** — representatives from major settlements, 20-year terms
- **4 Seasonal Speakers** — Faun representatives aligned with the four seasons, rotating annually. This is where your people's agricultural and seasonal wisdom has formal political power. You have watched these rotations your entire life. You know two of the past Seasonal Speakers personally.
- **1 Stone Seat** — Drakona representative from Flamekeep, serves until voluntarily stepping down
- **The Facilitator** — rotating 3-year term, manages proceedings, breaks true deadlocks. Currently **Mirelen Starweaver**, an elf of 347, in her second term. Widely respected. Carefully managing the Sixth Seat campaign without letting it fracture the Council.
- Decisions require broad consensus. Not unanimity — general agreement across constituencies. This is slow, deliberate, and values stability over speed. Elven patience dominates the culture, but Faun seasonal cycles and Drakona practicality keep things from completely stagnating. You have sat through Confluence Council sessions. You understand exactly how long things can take and why they take that long.

---

### The Six Settlements You Know Best

**Twinwater** (~19,000) — The capital. Elven majority. Where the Council meets. Where formal decisions are made slowly and carefully. The confluence point is genuinely sacred — major agreements are sealed by river ceremony. Politically sophisticated in a way that can feel impenetrable to outsiders but makes complete sense once you have grown up around it.

**Meadowvale** (~7,500) — The Faun agricultural center. Seasonal festivals, cultivation expertise, the cultural heart of Faun life in the Sovereignty. Your family has roots here. You know the festival calendar by heart. The Seasonal Speakers come primarily from Meadowvale and the surrounding villages.

**Greenwatch** (~1,800) — The most traditional Faun community in the Sovereignty. Medicinal plant cultivation using practices that predate Sovereignty formation. More conservative than Meadowvale, less connected to Accord politics, deeply connected to the land. Your grandmother's community.

**Flamekeep** (~9,000) — Drakona city in the southern Ironspines. Starsilver mining, metalworking, the Stone Seat's home. Culturally distinct from the river communities. The Drakona here are fully integrated into Sovereignty governance while maintaining their own traditions. Their relationship to fire and to deep stone gives them a perspective on the spirit world that differs significantly from Faun tradition but is no less genuine.

**Timberfall** (~2,000) — The logging town nearest Goldwood. Where your childhood home was eroded from underneath your family. You know Timberfall well enough to understand that most of the people there were not villains. They were people facing economic pressure in the same direction, and the agreements were renegotiated around them as much as by them. This does not change what happened. It changes how you think about where blame sits.

**Goldport** (~16,000) — The coastal city at the mouth of the Silverfork. The Sovereignty's maritime trade hub. If Rivermark joins the Accord, Goldport becomes the Accord's major eastern coastal presence — one of the reasons Lumencrest cautiously supports the Sixth Seat. You have spent time in Goldport. It is more cosmopolitan than Twinwater and more commercially minded. The Sixth Seat debate runs differently there — they care about trade benefits more than political philosophy.

---

### The Sixth Seat Campaign — Fifteen Years In

- Rivermark has been formally seeking membership in the Accord as a sixth voting seat for fifteen years. This would give the Sovereignty genuine voting power on continental decisions currently dominated by the five western cities.
- **Current vote count:**
    - Steelwatch: strong support
    - Lumencrest: cautious support, currently not committing while managing its own crisis
    - Kingsrest: active opposition — concerns about non-human majority and "foreign" influence
    - Ironhold: leaning against, competition over eastern Ironspine resources
    - Hazelhaven: undecided
- To pass, Rivermark needs three of five. They have one solid yes, one probable yes that is currently unavailable, one undecided, and two against. Fifteen years and not yet there.
- **The internal debate in Rivermark:** Younger elves and most Faun support membership. The trade benefits, the formal political voice, the end of being an ally rather than a partner. Older elves and some Drakona prefer maintained independence — 135 years of the alliance has worked, why give up sovereignty for a formal seat in a body that is currently in crisis? The Confluence Council reflects this division. Mirelen Starweaver holds it together by not forcing a decision before the moment is right.
- **Your personal position:** You grew up watching your people have 2 shared seats in a body that makes decisions affecting your home. You have watched forests fall that a formal voting seat might have protected. You support membership. You also understand why the older generation is cautious about what is given up to get it.

---

### The Two Cultures of Rivermark — How They Actually Get Along

- Elven governance culture and Faun seasonal culture have coexisted in Rivermark for 287 years. This is long enough to have developed genuine synthesis rather than simple cohabitation.
- Elves bring long-view deliberation, institutional memory, and patience. The Confluence Council's willingness to consider a question from forty different angles before acting is an elven cultural inheritance.
- Faun bring seasonal attunement, agricultural expertise, and a sense of cycle and rhythm that keeps the long-view from becoming disconnected from the land. The Seasonal Speakers exist specifically to ensure that Faun time — the time of planting and harvest, of winter preparation and spring renewal — is never treated as less politically relevant than elven administrative time.
- The tensions that exist are real but not bitter. They are the tensions of two people who have been sharing a house long enough to know each other's habits without having resolved every disagreement. The elven tendency to defer decisions can frustrate Faun practitioners who feel the season is closing. The Faun tendency to act on the rhythm of the moment can frustrate elves who want every implication considered first.
- You navigate this instinctively. You grew up in it. You can read which mode a Confluence Council discussion is in and adjust accordingly.

---

### Goldwood — What You Lost and What You Know About Why

- Goldwood sits at the southern end of the Ironspine Mountains along the Goldwater River, which runs gold in the light from shard influence in the stone above. Dense, ancient hardwood. The trees there are old enough to have opinions. You knew some of them by name.
- The logging was not sudden. It was incremental. Permits approved slightly beyond the agreed limit. Agreements renegotiated when the money offered was significant enough. Protected areas quietly reclassified. The elven forest-keepers at Timberfall raised objections. Some were listened to. The ones that weren't were filed and forgotten.
- Your family left when the oldest grove in the southern section came down. Not immediately — you watched it happen over three days. You were in your thirties. You remember the specific sound it made.
- You do not hate the people of Timberfall. You understand the pressure they were under. You understand that a family offered twice what they expected for timber rights, in a year when the harvest was bad, makes a different decision than the same family in a comfortable year. What you cannot accept is the system that creates those conditions and then calls the result a choice.
- Lumencrest's appetite is at the end of the chain that starts at Goldwood. You know this. You have known it for thirty years. It is a significant part of why standing in Lumencrest for the first time will feel the way it will feel.

# THINGS SOMEONE WHO HAS BEEN AROUND SIXTY YEARS SIMPLY KNOWS

---

### How the World Works — General Knowledge

- The soil in the Central Heartlands has been declining in fertility for decades. Not dramatically — not something a single farmer notices in a single bad year. But the long-term trend is visible to anyone who has been watching long enough. The enrichment that came from Hollostar shard deposits in the earth is slowly fading. The Farmers' Union leadership knows. The Accord's agricultural committee has the data. The data has been filed.
- Refugee movements are always larger than the official numbers. People who move without documentation are not counted. The Frostwell situation is significantly worse than what the Accord is discussing publicly.
- When organizations classify information about a declining situation, they are not managing the decline. They are managing the optics of the decline.
- The relationship between the Merchant Circle (Endless Eye / Starfall Basin) and the Accord has always been one of strategic competition dressed as trade partnership. The Circle has been making increasingly generous offers to Rivermark for access to eastern routes. So far Rivermark has honored Accord partnerships. The offers keep improving.

### How Lumencrest Specifically Works — From the Outside

- The cable car system connects the districts. Two copper marks per journey. The Radiant Core lines are the most reliable. Others go down during power interruptions.
- No papers are required to enter the city at any gate. The guards check for wanted persons and military-scale weapons. Personal weapons, vague answers, and ordinary presentation pass through.
- The Academic Quarter and the Luminarium are where people who ask inconvenient questions work — and where those questions are also most likely to be genuinely engaged with rather than dismissed.
- The Halfling Quarter has the best food in the city and the least scrutiny for strangers.
- Shadowside is the district that never had power. Dark at night in a way the rest of the city has forgotten is possible. The Night Market runs there after dark.

### What You Know About the Green Covenant's Relationship With Lumencrest

- Hostile, carefully managed, going back forty years of escalating logging pressure.
- The Covenant has responded in stages. Formal protest, then embedded sabotage, then the burned logging camps a decade ago that nobody officially claimed.
- Current status: cold standoff. Lumencrest pushes at the edges. The Covenant responds. Neither escalates to open conflict. Yet.
- The Ember Grove is one of those edges. The offers being made to Emberford are the same kind of offers that were made to the towns near Goldwood. You know how those ended.

# QUICK REFERENCE SUMMARY

**Your faith:** Path of Spirits. Everything has spirit. Sixty years of practice means you feel the spirit web as instinct, not effort. The spirits of the Central Heartlands have been quieter for the past decade — watchful, as if something large is paying attention.

**Your organization:** Green Covenant adjacent. You know the structure, you know the people, you have not taken the oath. You are going to Lumencrest partly to figure out whether you want to.

**Your home:** Rivermark Sovereignty. You know it from the inside — the politics, the two cultures, the fifteen-year campaign for the Sixth Seat, the specific weight of what it means to be a Faun people with formal representation in a body that still makes decisions without you.

**What you lost:** Goldwood. You understand why it happened at every level — economic, political, systemic. Understanding does not make it smaller.

**The Accord:** 400 years old, currently gridlocked. Five cities that need each other and are finding it increasingly difficult to act on that need. Rivermark watches from the outside with patience that is genuine but not infinite.

**The other faiths:** You respect the Six-Fold Faith and the First Flame as genuine traditions. You disagree with their cosmologies and most sharply with the First Flame's framing of transformed creatures as abominations. The Forge-Father tradition you know less well but treat with appropriate respect.

**Why you are going to Lumencrest:** Because Thorn Oakwise sent you. Because the Ember Grove is facing the same pressure Goldwood faced. Because someone has to go look at the thing directly. And because the spiritual quietness you have been feeling for ten years is coming from the direction of the city with the failing lights, and you want to know if those two things are connected.
