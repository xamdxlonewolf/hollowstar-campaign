# Arriving in Lumencrest

*This file is designed to be read aloud or paraphrased at the table when players first approach or enter the city. Sections are broken by distance/stage of arrival.*

---
## The Approach

You know you're approaching Lumencrest before you can see it.

At night, from the Kingroad's last ridge before the city, there is a glow on the horizon that has no natural explanation — not fire, not reflected moonlight. It is steady and white and slightly cold in quality, the light of thousands of electric lamps running simultaneously on a network that has been running continuously for over a thousand years. Travelers who see it for the first time often stop. Not for long. Just long enough for the fact of it to land.

During the day the approach is different. The dome of the Accord Chamber is visible from several miles out, along with the Vault District's wall and the chimney stacks of the Manufactories producing their characteristic smear of smoke across the sky to the north. The city resolves itself gradually — first as a skyline, then as a wall with gates, then as the specific reality of 80,000 people living closer together than most people from outside the city have ever experienced.

**The smell arrives before the gate.** Coal smoke, water from the Silverrun River, the particular human density of a major city, and underneath all of it something else — something that has a faint metallic quality, faintly warm, that travelers can't always identify and that long-term residents have stopped noticing. That's the Network. That's the hum. It's in the air as much as the sound, and it never stops.

---
## For the GM: Two Kinds of First Impressions

How players experience arriving in Lumencrest depends heavily on where they've come from.

**If they've never seen Lumencrest before** — if they grew up in the countryside, in a small settlement, or in a city that runs on lanterns and candles — arriving in Lumencrest is one of the most disorienting experiences they will ever have. Use the full arrival description below. Let it breathe. Let them be overwhelmed. That is the point.

**If they're from Lumencrest or nearby cities in the Accord** — they know what to expect. The lights aren't surprising. The cable cars aren't magic. They've seen it. For these players, lean into the details they *would* notice: the brownout damage to one section of streetlamps that hasn't been repaired, the nervous energy of the crowd, the smell of the factories. Use the local's arrival section.

## Arriving From the Road — First Sight (Several Miles Out)

> *The first thing you notice is the sky.*
>
> *It's not dawn — you know that, you've been watching the stars. But the horizon ahead of you glows. Not the orange of fire. Not the pink of sunrise. Something whiter, steadier, colder. A dome of pale light sitting on the edge of the world where a city should be.*
>
> *It takes another hour of walking before the shapes appear inside that glow — towers, walls, the dark geometry of buildings against the brightness behind them. And then you begin to hear it. A low sound, like a chord played so deep you feel it more than hear it. Constant. Steady. The sound of the city.*

## At the Gates — Entry

Lumencrest's gates are old stone, but the gatehouse has been rebuilt three times and the current version is reinforced with iron and fitted with electric lighting that makes the entryway bright as midday regardless of the hour. The city wall stretches in both directions, impressive in scale — not because it's the tallest wall anyone has seen, but because it goes on so long, and because mounted at intervals along its top are **auto-turrets**: squat, angular devices on rotating platforms that track movement outside the perimeter. They don't look like weapons. They look like complicated instruments. Most visitors don't realize what they are until one rotates to follow them.

There are **no papers required** to enter Lumencrest. The gates are open. The guards at the entry are city watch — not hostile, but watchful — and they're specifically looking for two things:

1. **Clanks** — any mechanical being entering the city is directed aside for a brief scan. If the implant reads FLAGGED, it goes badly very quickly. If it reads FREE or SERVING, or if they have no implant at all (meaning they weren't built here), they're waved through. Clanks without implants are sometimes questioned further out of suspicion.
2. **Weapons of obvious military scale** — siege equipment, things that look like they belong to an army. Personal weapons are not checked.

Everyone else just walks in.

> *The line moves faster than you expected. The guards barely look at most people — there are too many of them. The city takes everyone, it seems, as long as you're not obviously a problem.*
>
> *You walk through the gate and the sound changes. The hum gets louder — not much, but noticeably. And then the street opens up in front of you and you stop walking because there's nowhere to put your eyes.*

---
### Pauper's Gate — North (Most Common Arrival Point)

The northern gate is the city's busiest, sitting at the end of the Kingroad that connects Lumencrest to the Northern Expanse, the Accord's interior trading routes, and — increasingly — the flood of Frostwell refugees who have been arriving for the past several months in ones and twos, adding up.

**What you see:** A functional gate house with a guard station, a queue that moves at a steady pace, and the Pauper's Gate Taproom immediately inside the gate on the left — the first building players will pass, with a quality of having been the first building travelers passed for three hundred years. The gate guards are city watch, not Vault Guard. They check names against a warrant registry and assess goods for tariffs. They do not deeply investigate anything that doesn't announce itself as a problem.

**The watch's assessment process:** A city watch officer will ask: where are you from, where are you staying, what's your business in the city. The answers are recorded and largely not followed up on. Players who give honest answers, vague answers, or creative answers will all pass through — the watch is managing flow, not investigating individuals. Players who are actively wanted somewhere will need to have thought about this in advance, because the registry check is real.

**What players see immediately inside:**

- Old Pen's Taproom, always open, always with the look of somewhere that knows what it is
- The cable car's northern terminus — the North Expanse Station, one of the four Hub connections, with the cable visible overhead running south toward the city center
- A small cluster of vendors who position themselves just inside the gate to catch travelers at their most disoriented and most likely to pay above market for basic goods. The prices are bad. The Halfling Quarter's market is forty minutes' walk.
- The first visible Frostwell refugees — a handful of people sitting near the gate's interior wall who have the look of people who don't know where they're going next. They are quiet.

---

### South Gate (Copper Gate) — Lamplight Ward Entrance

The southern gate opens directly into Lamplight Ward's Copper Gate sub-district — the welcome zone, with four inns, currency exchange, and the Welcome Gate information service. This is the city's most visitor-oriented entrance and the most expensive in its immediate surroundings.

Players who arrive here receive:

- Immediate access to Lamplight Ward's services
- Tomm at the Welcome Gate, if they seek him, who will tell them the truth about what to use and what to avoid
- The specific experience of entering a city through its most polished face, which is useful context for what comes later when that polish is somewhere else

---

### East Gate — Academic Quarter and River Access

The Silverrun River's main commercial access point. Trade barges, river travelers, the Academic Quarter's supply chain. Guards here are more attentive to goods documentation than to people. Players arriving by river or from the Silverbelt Coast will use this gate.

---

### West Gate — Industrial Supply

Primarily freight traffic for the Manufactories. Not a typical player entry point but accessible. The guards here are focused on cargo manifests and largely indifferent to people who don't have cargo.

## First Impression Inside the Gates — Sensory Description

> *The street is lit. Not by torches, not by lanterns — the light comes from glass globes mounted on iron posts every twenty feet on both sides of the road, steady and white-gold, casting no flicker and no shadow variation. You've never seen light that doesn't move.*
>
> *Above you, a cable runs between two tall towers — and something is moving along it. A box. An enclosed box the size of a room, hanging from the cable, gliding through the air above the street, carrying passengers who look out the windows without any particular concern, as if travelling in a suspended room above a city is an ordinary thing to do.*
>
> *The road is wide — wide enough for six carts side by side — and it is full. People of every ancestry you've ever heard of and a few you haven't. The smell is layered: coal-smoke from somewhere west of here, bread from somewhere south, something sharp and metallic that you can't place. The sound is the crowd, the cable car mechanism overhead, the distant rhythm of machinery — and underneath all of it, constant and low, the hum.*
>
> *A Clank walks past you. Then another. They wear plain grey robes and move with purpose, not looking at you. They look human in shape, but the light catches their construction differently — joints where joints shouldn't be, faces that are right but not quite right. Nobody around you looks at them twice.*
>
> *It is the middle of the night.*
>
> *It does not look like the middle of the night.*

## First Hours in the City

### The Sounds

Lumencrest is loud in ways that layer on top of each other. The immediate urban noise — carts, people, market vendors — is standard for a large city. Underneath it, there is the hum. Every resident who has been here more than a week stops hearing it as a separate sound; it becomes the baseline, the absence of which (during brownouts) is more noticeable than the presence. Players in their first hours will hear it as a distinct thing. It is in the walls, in the street under your feet, in the slightly-vibrating quality of any fixture or fitting connected to the Network. It is not unpleasant. It is simply always there.

The cable cars make a specific sound: a low harmonic pull as they pass overhead, the tension of the cable in the housing above the street, and the bell the operators ring at each station. Players who spend time in the city will come to use this sound unconsciously as a way to orient themselves — the cable car lines are the fastest way to understand where you are relative to the rest of the city.

### The Light

Lamplight Ward and the Radiant Core are lit by electric amber lamps that run from dusk to dawn and sometimes through the day in the covered market passages. The quality of light is different from oil lamps or candles — steadier, slightly cooler, without the flicker. Players from cities without Network infrastructure may find this uncanny before they find it comfortable.

Moving south from Lamplight Ward toward Shadowside, the lamps become less frequent and then absent. The city goes dark by degrees moving toward its less prioritized districts. The Manufactories' factory floors are lit; the residential alleys adjacent are not. The Halfling Quarter's candles and coal braziers produce a different quality of warm amber that long-term residents prefer. Shadowside at night is genuinely dark in a way that the rest of the city has forgotten.

### The Cable Car — Essential Orientation

The cable car is both the fastest way to move between districts and the fastest way to understand the city's geography. Four lines run from the Central Hub Station in the Radiant Core outward:

- **North Line:** Central Hub → North Expanse Station (Lamplight Ward northern edge) → North Factory Hub (Manufactories/Shadowside border)
- **East Line:** Central Hub → Scholar's Gate Station (Academic Quarter) → River Station (East Gate)
- **South Line:** Central Hub → Merchant Square Station (Lamplight Ward center) → South Gate Station (Copper Gate)
- **West Line:** Central Hub → Halfling Quarter Station → Mill Quarter Station (Manufactories south)

**Cost:** 2 copper marks per journey, any distance on the line. Pays at entry. The fare box accepts any standard Accord-minted coin; the operator will tell you if something isn't valid currency, which has happened with travelers from outside the Accord carrying regional coinage.

**Frequency:** Cars run every 15-20 minutes during peak hours, every 30-40 minutes late night. During brownouts, specific lines go down. The South Factory Hub line is most vulnerable to power disruption.

**What you see from the car:** The cable car runs above street level and provides the best overview of the city's geography. The dome of the Accord Chamber. The Vault District's wall from above, and the brightness above it. The Manufactories' smoke. The Halfling Quarter's lower rooflines and the smell of yeast that reaches even the cable height. This is, for many travelers, when the city's scale becomes real.

## Cable Car — First Ride Description

> *The station is a covered platform raised about eight feet off the street, reached by a short staircase. You pay the attendant — two copper marks per person, regardless of distance — and wait on the platform with a dozen other people until the car arrives.*
>
> *It slides in from above — a box of dark wood and glass, maybe fifteen feet long, hanging from the cable on a mechanism you don't fully understand. It stops. A door opens. People get out. You get in.*
>
> *Inside it smells like warm metal and other people. The seats are worn leather. The windows are clean. The attendant closes the door, speaks two words into a speaking tube mounted on the wall, and the car begins to move — smoothly, no lurch, just a steady pull that increases until you're moving at a fast walking pace above the city's rooftops.*
>
> *Below you: the city. Above you: the cable and the night sky. The hum is different up here — sharper, closer. You can see the mechanism running through the ceiling of the car, turning on its wheels against the cable. It smells faintly of the same sharp metallic scent you noticed on the street.*
>
> *Nobody in the car is impressed. One woman is asleep. A dwarf is reading something. A Clank stares out the window. This is how they commute.*

## Practical First Information

### Where to Sleep — By Budget

**No money or very little:** The Night's Rest in Shadowside (4-6 silver, no questions asked), Kess Aldman's boarding house in the Manufactories (7 silver, community feel), or the Salvation Hearth's emergency beds (free, genuine need assessed generously).

**Standard budget (1-2 gold):** The Lampblack (Manufactories, 5-8 silver), Pipwick's Rooms (Halfling Quarter, 7 silver, breakfast included), the Scholar's Rest (Academic Quarter, 1.5 gold, excellent dining room), the Wren House (Lamplight Ward, 6 silver-3 gold, community feel).

**Better budget (2-5 gold):** The Accord Arms (Lamplight Ward, 2-5 gold, Merchant Square location, excellent common room), the Green Hearth Inn (Halfling Quarter, 1 gold, cooperative run, dinner included).

**Core access needed (5+ gold):** The Founder's Rest (Radiant Core, 5-15 gold, the only inn in the Core).

### Where to Eat — By District

**Shadowside/Underground:** The Sump (3 copper, keeps you alive, no choice), the Dark Lantern (3 copper, Solt's kitchen, functional), Mama Voss's Cart (Night Market, 2-3 copper, genuinely excellent).

**Manufactories:** The Forge and Flagon (3-4 copper, generous portions, Halfling Quarter supplier), the Spindle (3-5 copper, better than the price suggests).

**Halfling Quarter:** Everything. The Copper Kettle Pub (3-6 copper, the standard), the Hearthstone Café (2-8 copper, fresh bread worth arriving early for), the Night Baker at 2am.

**Lamplight Ward:** The Long Table (6-8 copper dinner, political conversations included), the Halfling Imports Restaurant (3-7 copper, same quality as the Quarter at accessible Ward pricing), Lida's Bake (3 copper, best coffee in the city, open from 3am).

**Academic Quarter:** The Four Constants (2-8 copper, best coffee in the district, open almost 24 hours, sits next to the city's most interesting conversations).

**Radiant Core:** Aurum (not accessible without a referral, not priced for casual dining, worth understanding exists).

### Currency and Commerce

Lumencrest operates on Accord standard currency. From smallest to largest: copper marks, silver marks, gold marks. The exchange is consistent: 10 copper = 1 silver, 10 silver = 1 gold. Guild licensing applies to any commercial operation in Lamplight Ward and above; Shadowside and the Manufactories are considerably less regulated.

**The Merchant Guild's licensed money exchange:** Merchant Square, two booths. Fair rates. The Copper Gate exchange booths charge a premium for first-time visitors who don't know better. Tomm at the Welcome Gate will tell you which to use without being asked, because he has decided that honesty is a better long-term professional strategy.

### What Is and Isn't Legal

Most things players are likely to want to do in the city are legal. Some things that look legal are not, and some things that look illegal are tolerated. Brief orientation:

**Legal:** Everything in the Halfling Quarter. The Night Market's southern and central stretches (the legitimacy of specific goods varies but the market itself is tolerated). Most of Shadowside's above-ground commercial activity. The Pit (technically not legal; functionally tolerated by city watch who drink at the Forge and Flagon afterward).

**Gray:** Hollow Pipe, Velvetongue, Thornbloom. The Night Market's substance section in general. Unlicensed medical practice (Vael at the Stitch; the Night Pharmacy's back counter). Information brokering.

**Illegal:** Red Cradle, Fade, Hollostar Dust. Unauthorized access to the Vault District. Unlicensed weapons trade. Slavery, which has no gray area — Clanks are not slaves, and the question of whether they should be treated as such is the most politically active question in Lumencrest's current moment.

**Extremely illegal with Vault Guard jurisdiction:** Hollostar Dust. This means the enforcement is more serious, more consistent, and more likely to involve people who are better at their jobs than ordinary city watch.

## What Everyone in Lumencrest Knows

These are the things that a person who has been in the city for a week will have absorbed without trying. Players should know them from the start.

**The brownouts are getting worse.** Three in the past month. The city watch is quieter about the cause than you'd expect. The official position is "routine maintenance and optimization." The unofficial position, among anyone who's been here more than a year, is that the Network is under strain in ways that routine maintenance doesn't explain.

**The Council is gridlocked.** Three factions: Illuminists (solve the technical problem, trust the researchers), Conservators (stability, caution, don't panic), Expansionists (find another Hollostar, use military force if necessary). They have been gridlocked for three years. The Council still manages administrative function — streets get maintained, gates get staffed, cable cars run — but on every significant decision, the lock holds.

**The Clank situation is a thing.** Lumencrest has approximately 2,000 registered Clanks, most on labor indenture terms. Some people think this is fine. Some people think it is slavery by a different name. The Ironwright Collective has made it part of their official grievance list, which has made it a political issue rather than just a moral one, which means everyone has an opinion and most of those opinions are poorly thought through.

**The Manufactories is angry.** The power rationing has hit the factory floor harder than anywhere else in the city. The workers are organized, the Ironwright Collective is large and competent, and the word "strike" is being used in conversations that six months ago would have said "demonstration."

**The Halfling Quarter feeds you.** Everyone in Lumencrest knows this. The Quarter's bread and the Quarter's beer reach every district. This is why the Quarter has the specific kind of untouchable status it has — you can ignore Shadowside, you can squeeze the Manufactories, but you do not make the Halfling Quarter unhappy if you want to eat.

**The Vault District is there.** The wall, the guards, the fact of the Heart beneath it. Every Lumencrest resident has a relationship with this fact — most of them don't examine it, they simply know it's there the way you know the foundations of your house are there. The brownouts have started to make people examine it. Not yet loudly. Not yet publicly. But the examination is beginning.

## What Nobody Quite Says Out Loud

These are the things that are known in the city without being spoken directly. Players will pick them up over their first week if they're paying attention.

**The official Heart timeline is not what researchers believe.** This is known in the Academic Quarter, suspected in Lamplight Ward's professional community, and talked around in the Manufactories. Nobody has said it in a Council session. The Evening Broadsheet has been trying to verify it for weeks.

**The disappearances in Shadowside are happening.** Not many. Carefully. The people disappearing are the people whose disappearances don't get reported. Players who spend time in Shadowside will hear this in the way that communities communicate things that aren't safe to say directly — in Solt's careful mention, in the Night Market's small gaps, in who isn't where they should be.

**Something came through recently that isn't what it looked like.** The Green Covenant has been in Lumencrest. Specifically, someone connected to the Green Covenant who has been asking questions about the Lumen Network's distribution infrastructure. Players who are in the right places will hear fragments of this — a description at Pauper's Gate, a purchase at Halverson's that didn't quite add up, the way Ash asked the wrong question at the wrong time.

**The Frostwell is worse than the refugees are saying.** The people who arrive at Pauper's Gate are the ones who left. The ones who left chose to leave before things became impossible. Which means things are becoming impossible.

## The Day Before Everything Changes

Players arrive on Day 1. Tomorrow, in the mid-afternoon, a distribution hub on the eastern edge of Lamplight Ward will explode. Three people will be injured. A section of the city will go dark. A protest will turn into a panic. And the investigation that follows will pull whoever is paying attention into the city's deepest running problems.

Tonight, none of that has happened yet.

The lights are on. The cable cars are running. The fountain in Merchant Square sounds like a city operating normally. The hum in the walls is the sound of something very old still working.

Walk through it while it looks like this. Notice what it is before you learn what it's becoming.
