# House Vael and the Unseen Guild — GM Reference

## The Hidden Architecture of Lumencrest's Crisis

_This document covers House Vael's three-generation strategy, Lyra Vael's Shard Division operation, the Unseen Guild's full continental and Lumencrest structure, and how the two organizations relate to each other and to the campaign's Act One threads. This is the most sensitive GM-only document in the Lumencrest series — none of this should reach players directly. It should reach them through evidence, observation, and the slow accumulation of correctly interpreted details._

_Cross-reference [[Lumencrest_NPC_Index]] for character profiles, [[Lumencrest_Timeline_Act1]] for when specific elements become visible, and the Campaign Arc document for how this connects to Acts Two and Three._

## PART ONE — HOUSE VAEL

### The Three-Generation Strategy

House Vael is not a Lumencrest family. It is an Empire family that made a strategic decision three generations ago and has been executing it with a patience that most organizations cannot sustain across a single generation, let alone three.

**The founding decision:** Vael's great-grandmother, **Sera Vael**, identified that the continent's power structure was organized around Montauseau and the Empire's administrative center — but that real power, specifically the power of the Lumen Network and the economic activity it enabled, was consolidating in Lumencrest. She spent the last decade of her life establishing a foothold: a commercial presence, a marriage alliance with a minor Lumencrest merchant family, and a document that has been passed to each Vael heir since. The document is a strategic assessment. It concludes: _When the Heart fails, whoever is positioned to stabilize Lumencrest will hold the continent._

**The middle generation:** Vael's grandfather, **Aros Vael**, converted the commercial presence into political presence. He cultivated relationships with every significant faction in Lumencrest over thirty years, took no strong positions, gave large charitable donations to the Salvation Hearth and the Academic Quarter's scholarship fund, and arranged his son's education at the Luminarium. By the time he died, the Vael name was associated with stability, generosity, and a kind of patrician reasonableness that no faction found threatening. This was the point.

**The current generation:**

**Sevan Vael** (54) inherited a perfect position and has spent eleven years on the Council not spending it. This requires its own discipline — the discipline of not acting when every instinct says the moment is right, because the moment isn't right yet. He votes consistently enough to be legible (Conservator-aligned independent, broadly stabilizing) and inconsistently enough to be valuable (three key votes in the past four years went in unexpected directions that turned out to serve the House's positioning). He is the best-regarded moderate voice in the Council and has achieved this entirely through strategy rather than principle, which he would not describe as dishonest and which is not entirely honest.

**What Sevan knows and doesn't know:**

- Knows: The Heart's actual decline rate (through connections at the Vault Guard that predate Coldwell's tenure). The Expansionist vote count (through the same channels as Demi Wren). The rough shape of Lyra's activities (not the specific content — he has chosen not to receive specific content).
- Doesn't know: The Shard Division's methodology. The number of subjects. What the experiments are producing. He knows Lyra is involved in something that intersects with the Guild and that involves shard material. He has made a deliberate decision not to know more, because what he knows he can credibly claim ignorance of and what he doesn't know he cannot be asked to act on.
- His decision about Lyra: If her operation produces a catastrophic failure — public exposure, large-scale harm that can be attributed to her — he will sacrifice her to protect the House. He has thought this through. He has concluded she would consider it the correct strategic decision in his position. He is probably right about this.

**Lyra Vael** (25) is operating almost entirely independently of her father. She communicates with him through a social fiction they both maintain: she is an ambitious junior House representative learning the political environment. Their actual relationship is two people who understand each other completely and have divided the House's activities into the legitimate and the not, and maintain a shared agreement not to examine the boundary too closely.

She is the most dangerous person in Lumencrest. Not because she is cruel (though she is indifferent to harm in others) but because she is intelligent, patient, and has identified something real: shard transformation, if controlled, produces capabilities that cannot be matched by any ordinary means. She is three years ahead of where anyone else is in understanding this. If she is right about what she's building toward — an elite of shard-transformed operatives serving House Vael's interests — the House's position when Lumencrest fractures becomes essentially unassailable.

She could also be wrong. The experiments have a significant mortality rate. The transformation is not fully controllable. Several subjects who survived are being "contained" rather than utilized because their transformation produced something unpredictable. She is aware of all of this and has concluded that the data she's accumulating is worth the cost, and that the deaths are an inevitable research cost rather than a moral problem. She has been telling herself this for two years and she has not yet found a reason to stop.

### House Vael's Lumencrest Operations — Active Threads

**The Sevan layer (legitimate, visible):**

- Council presence and moderate reputation
- Financial leverage over several Manufactories-adjacent guild shareholders through debt arrangements made during the Unification Wars era (these are legitimate debts; Sevan calls them "relationships")
- Empire connections that provide intelligence about how Lumencrest is perceived from outside the Accord — information he shares selectively with Mira Ashton and Theron Ashwick, which gives him influence with both
- The Conditional Vote: Sevan has agreed to vote for Ashwick's military authorization motion if specific conditions are written into the language — conditions that appear to limit scope but are drafted to not actually do so. This arrangement was made at Aurum, witnessed only by Cass Vorn, who has noted it in their private records. This is the arrangement Demi Wren has been trying to identify for six months and hasn't quite confirmed.

**The Lyra layer (operational, hidden):**

- The Shard Division funding and oversight (see Part Two below)
- The Isle of Broken Sphere arrangement with Lady Petra Caldren — a financial partnership to develop the Isle as a resource, which Lyra is pursuing because she has assessed that controlling the Dragon Turtle's territory (though she does not know it is a Dragon Turtle) when it stabilizes could be significant
- Quiet contact with the corrupted Starfall Seekers, separate from the Guild's contact — Lyra is cultivating a direct relationship with Crane's group that doesn't run through Mira Voss, building toward eventual independent access to Seeker research data

**The family layer (structural, invisible):**

- The three-generation documentation that Sevan holds and that Lyra has read — the strategic assessment and the founding decision
- Empire contacts who have been quietly assessing what a stabilized Lumencrest under Vael influence would mean for the Empire's continental position. The Empire is not involved in Lumencrest's current affairs; it is watching with the patience of an institution that thinks in decades.

### How to Play House Vael at the Table

**Sevan:** The long game made flesh. He is in no hurry. He is never rattled. He says the correct thing for the situation, not the true thing. Players who interact with him in the first twenty sessions of Act One should find him genuinely helpful — he will provide small pieces of useful context, offer introductions, facilitate minor access. He is building a picture of who the players are and whether they're useful. He is not yet deciding whether they're a threat.

The moment his assessment changes is when players touch the Shard Division. Once they are demonstrably close to Lyra's operation, Sevan's behavior shifts — not dramatically, not visibly, but specifically. He stops initiating contact. The small helpful gestures stop. He is repositioning.

**Lyra:** Players will not meet her directly in Act One's first half. They will see evidence of her — the financial records, the Guild connection, the shard supply chain. When they do meet her, she will be exactly what she presents as: charming, intelligent, ambitious in ways the Core finds admirable. The gap between what she presents and what she is should emerge from evidence rather than her behavior in the room. She never breaks character. She never needs to.

**The exposure problem:** Exposing House Vael requires distinguishing between Sevan (complicit by inaction, possibly prosecutable, definitely politically ruinous) and Lyra (operationally guilty, directly prosecutable). Exposing Lyra without exposing Sevan: possible, and leaves the House diminished but intact, with Sevan able to claim he didn't know. Exposing both: destabilizes the most credible moderate voice on the Council at a moment when the moderate voice is the only thing slowing the Expansionist vote. There is no clean answer — intentional.

## PART TWO — THE UNSEEN GUILD

### Continental Structure — What Players Will Eventually Know

The Unseen Guild is headquartered in Sablemark. It has a Council seat. The Patron — current identity unknown, changes approximately every decade — represents the Guild's interests in the Sablemark Council. This is an open secret that everyone understands and nobody officially acknowledges, because the alternative to organized crime is disorganized crime, and the Guild keeps things orderly.

**How the Guild works:** It is a logistics and services organization. It does not manufacture; it facilitates. It moves things that cannot be moved officially, provides services that cannot be officially provided, and takes a percentage at each transaction point. Its value proposition is reliability — when you use the Guild, you get what you paid for, done correctly, without complications. This reliability is the Guild's most important product and the reason it has survived and grown for 200 years.

**The hierarchy (simplified):**

- The Patron (Sablemark; strategic direction; public representation)
- Regional Coordinators (each major Accord city and significant region; operational leadership)
- Deputies (logistics and security; bridge between regional and street level)
- Distribution Network (independent operators under Guild tolerance; the visible trade layer)

**The separation principle:** The Guild maintains strict information separation between levels. Regional coordinators don't know the identities of street-level dealers. Deputies don't know the full extent of operations above them. This is not merely operational security — it is the Guild's ethical framework, such as it is. Mira Voss knows everything in the Lumencrest operation. A Night Market distributor knows their contact and their product source. Nothing else.

---

### The Lumencrest Operation — Standard Division

**Regional Coordinator:** Mira Voss (half-elf, 41). Brilliant, cold, transactional. She has run this operation for nine years. She is not Sablemark-born — she came up through the Guild's eastern network, which means she views the Lumencrest operation with the assessment of an outsider who has been here long enough to own it.

**What the standard operation covers:**

_Substance distribution:_ Full supply chain management for Red Cradle, Fade, Velvetongue, Thornbloom, and Hollostar Dust. The Guild does not produce any of these in Lumencrest (with the partial exception of Fade, where it funds the Manufactories facility). It imports, stores, and distributes. Independent retailers (Renn Aldous, Maren Solt, the Night Market distributors, the Quiet Market) operate under Guild tolerance.

_Warehousing:_ Two locations. The Manufactories basement facility (Clank-staffed, under cover of a defunct storage operation that technically still files Labour Board paperwork). The Shadowside eastern-edge cooperage basement (behind a legitimate cooperage front that was purchased for this purpose seven years ago and continues operating to maintain the cover).

_Import routes:_ Silverrun River barge traffic (primary, for most substances and goods). Pauper's Gate (secondary; lower scrutiny than east gate; used for smaller volume). The pre-city tunnel passages beneath the Maze Quarter (exclusively for Hollostar Dust; highest security, highest value).

_The Halfling Quarter unwitting cover:_ A merchant house in the Halfling Quarter has been facilitating river transport for the Guild for six years under the belief that they are shipping Accord-legal herbal goods. The actual contents have never been inspected. The merchant house principals are not criminals; they are people who were given a straightforward-seeming contract and have maintained it without examination. Elder Tommas Wickham has not identified this connection, which is unusual enough that Mira Voss considers it evidence that the cover is genuinely clean.

_Information brokerage:_ The Guild maintains contacts within the city's administrative apparatus who receive regular payments for specific operational intelligence (gate rotation schedules, inspection timetables, enforcement priorities). These contacts are not named here — GMs should place them where they serve the campaign best, with the constraint that at least one should be in a position that would surprise players when revealed.

_Contract services:_ The Guild provides these in Lumencrest but they are not widely advertised. Intimidation, problematic removal, targeted theft. Mira Voss has assessed the current political climate as too volatile for contract assassination — too many parties watching, too much chance of attribution. This position may change as Act One progresses.

### The Shard Division — Separate Command Structure

**What it is:** Not the Guild's standard model. A specific operation that Mira Voss created at Lyra Vael's instigation, running through some of the same infrastructure but with a completely separate command chain. Even within the Guild, only Mira and The Collector know its full scope.

**The stated objective:** Determine whether Hollostar shard transformation varies by ancestry, whether it can be directed or controlled, and whether the results can be weaponized into enhanced Guild operatives. This is Mira's framing. Lyra's framing is different: she wants to understand what shard transformation does and eventually apply it selectively. The Guild's interest is operatives. Lyra's interest is power. Both interests have produced the same operation.

**The corrupted Seeker connection:** Three Starfall Seeker researchers provide shard samples in exchange for the Guild's experimental data. They want to understand shard effects on living beings. The Guild is doing the experimentation the Seekers cannot officially perform.

- **Aldis Crane** (52, human) — Most senior of the three. Has genuine academic standing. Has been telling himself the data justifies the method for 18 months and finds this increasingly unconvincing. He is the weak link — not because he'll confess, but because his behavioral changes are becoming visible to colleagues who knew him before.
- **The other two:** Not named; placed by GM. One provides shard transport (academic field work provides cover for accessing Starfall Basin material). One provides the analytical framework the Guild uses to interpret the transformation data.

**The Collector:** Identity unknown even within the Guild's Lumencrest structure. Mira knows. Nobody else does. The Collector:

- Has been operating for two years without being identified
- Knows Shadowside better than city watch does — at the level of specific passages, specific routines, specific people
- Selects subjects by specific criteria: vulnerability (refugees, people without social networks that will notice), availability (present in specific locations at specific times), and health (the experiments have higher success rates with specific physiological profiles)
- Is not cruel. This is worth stating because it would be easier if they were. The operation is professional. The disappearances are planned carefully, executed without drama, designed to leave as little trace as possible. The subjects are not tortured — they are exposed to shard material under clinical conditions. Most of them die. The ones who don't are changed.

**Revealing the Collector:** The identity is a campaign-specific decision for the GM. Possible candidates: someone already present in the district documents who fits the profile, someone introduced specifically for this role, or someone the players have been interacting with in apparent good faith. The reveal should feel inevitable in retrospect — the Collector's criteria and methods, visible in the disappearance pattern, should point to someone who had the access, the knowledge, and the cover. The character needs to know Shadowside "better than city watch does" — which suggests long-term presence in the district.

**The experiment outcomes:**

- Mortality rate: approximately 65% across all subjects. Higher for non-human ancestries in early experiments; the methodology has been refined.
- "Useful" transformations: Approximately 12% of subjects survive with controlled capabilities that the Guild can assess as deployable. These subjects are currently held in the Guild's Manufactories warehouse facility's lower level. There are currently four of them.
- "Contained" transformations: Approximately 10% of subjects survive with capabilities that are uncontrolled, dangerous, or simply unpredictable. Three are being contained in a location not documented in this file — GMs should establish this location.
- Standard mortality: The remaining subjects died during or shortly after shard exposure.

**The transformed subjects currently held:** Not named here — these are Act One discoveries that should arrive through player investigation. When players find them, the experience should be significant: people who were members of Shadowside's community, or refugees, who are now something different. Not monsters. Changed people. The question of what to do with them — not as a tactical problem but as an ethical one — is one the campaign should sit with.

### The Guild's Relationship with the Hollostar Dust Supply

The Guild's Dust supply chain uses the pre-city tunnel passages beneath the Maze Quarter as its primary import route. This is the same tunnel network that connects to the Vault District's underground complex.

The Shard Division uses the same tunnel network to move subjects from Shadowside to the containment facility. These are not identical routes, but they share infrastructure — specifically the section beneath the Maze Quarter's deepest sections.

**The consequence:** Sera Coldwell's tunnel anomaly file documents activity in the pre-city passages at a frequency inconsistent with recreational Dust supply alone. She has not yet attributed this to two separate operations using the same route — she has been assuming a single operation with unusual volume. When players bring her evidence that clarifies the picture, the two operations separating in her analysis changes what she understands about the scale.

**The supply crisis:** The Shard Division's material consumption has reduced the supply of shard material available for Dust production. The Guild is aware of the tension — Mira Voss has had one conversation with Lyra about it, in which Lyra made clear that the Shard Division's requirements take priority and that the Dust supply chain should adapt. This has produced the cut-product problem that is currently endangering dependent users in Shadowside.

Mira Voss is unhappy about this. She considers it bad business to allow a supply chain to deteriorate in a way that damages the customer base. She has not, however, overruled Lyra on it, because Lyra is her most significant patron and the Shard Division is producing results that Mira has assessed as too valuable to risk through a funding dispute.

### The Guild Everywhere — Act Two Presence

The Unseen Guild is not a Lumencrest problem. It is a continental organization that players will encounter across all of Act Two's expedition locations. Brief notes on Guild presence in each location:

**Northern Expanse / Frostwell Border:** The Guild has been active here since the refugee movement began, for the obvious reason that mass displacement produces exactly the vulnerable population the Shard Division uses. The Collector's pause in Lumencrest operations has redirected activity here. Players who were tracking the Shadowside disappearances and follow the thread north will find evidence of the same operational signature in the refugee movement.

Additionally: the Northern Expanse trade routes carry Guild product. The cities of Celebrantford and Ironmere each have a Guild tolerance arrangement for specific goods — not the full Lumencrest operation, but a Guild-connected distribution point for substances and information brokerage.

**The Glittering Marshes:** Someone has been trying to find alternative access routes into the deep marsh that bypass the Ribbet guide requirement. This is Guild-adjacent — specifically, the corrupted Seeker researchers who want direct shard cluster access have been using Guild logistics to fund and equip access attempts. The Ribbet know someone has been trying; they don't know who.

**The Verdant Crescent:** Thornwall, the radical wing's Heartgrove contact, has a Guild connection that is not direct — they have used Guild communication infrastructure for the radical wing's coordination without fully understanding the nature of what they were using. This means the Guild has fragmentary knowledge of the radical wing's operations that it has not acted on because the political disruption in Lumencrest is, from the Guild's perspective, broadly useful to destabilizing the environment in ways that benefit the standard operation.

**The Isle of the Broken Sphere:** Lyra Vael's arrangement with Lady Petra Caldren gives the Guild a financial interest in the Isle's development. The Guild doesn't know what the Isle is; Lyra doesn't know either. But when the Dragon Turtle becomes public knowledge, the Guild's financial interest means Mira Voss will have opinions about what happens to it.

**Sablemark:** The campaign's Act Two may take players to Sablemark if they follow the Guild thread to its source. The Patron — whoever they are in this campaign's current cycle — is aware of the Lumencrest operation's complications and has been receiving reports. Whether the Patron is an ally, a neutral, or an additional antagonist is a GM decision. The Patron's Council seat and the Guild's "criminal order" function in Sablemark means that disrupting the Guild there has city-wide consequences, not just organizational ones.

**Frostvault:** The Starfall Seeker outpost in Frostvault has been infiltrated by the corrupted Seeker researchers to some degree — they have access to the outpost's communication infrastructure, which is how they've been passing shard samples and data in ways that look like normal Seeker research correspondence. Players who are in Frostvault and paying attention to the outpost's communication records will find the thread.

### Running the Guild at the Table

**The standard operation:** Background texture with specific consequences. The substance trade affects people players care about. The information brokerage means that things players do in the city are, occasionally, known by people who shouldn't know them. The Guild's presence should feel like water — everywhere, beneath the visible surface, only apparent when something specific makes it visible.

**The Shard Division:** The campaign's emotional core in Act One. The disappearances are personal. The transformed subjects, when players find them, should be people — not statistics. The question the players will have to sit with is: what do you do when you find four people in a warehouse who survived something that killed dozens of others and are now something that doesn't map to ordinary categories of person or threat?

**Mira Voss — when players reach her:** She will not panic. She will not be dramatic. She will assess the situation and respond proportionally. She has been managing complications for nine years and the specific complication of people investigating her operation is not new. Her response will be: what can be salvaged, what needs to be cut loose, and what does cutting it loose require. The Collector is theoretically cuttable — the Collector knows this and has made provisions. Lyra Vael is not cuttable; she is the funding source. This constraint shapes Mira's options in ways that players can leverage if they understand it.

**The Collector — when players identify them:** This is a significant scene. The Collector's identity should feel inevitable in retrospect. How the Collector responds to being identified depends on who they are — but the baseline is: professional, not panicked, focused on what the identification means operationally rather than emotionally. They will not confess. They will not apologize. They will assess whether the players are an asset, a neutral, or a problem to be managed.

## THE CONNECTION — How House Vael and the Guild Relate

Lyra Vael and Mira Voss have a peer relationship that is not a friendship and is not a normal business arrangement. They are two people who have made the same fundamental assessment about the world — that power is the only real thing and that the people who understand this are the only ones worth taking seriously — and who have found each other useful.

**The power dynamic:** Lyra provides funding and political protection. Mira provides operational capability and infrastructure. Neither is fully in charge of the arrangement. Both understand this and have calibrated their behavior accordingly.

**The tension:** Mira is running a decades-old professional operation that succeeds through discipline and reliability. The Shard Division is disruptive to that operation — it is drawing attention through the supply chain complications, it is creating liabilities (the contained subjects), and it has a timeline that Mira finds opaque. She wants results. She wants results to mean a deployable asset she can explain to her Sablemark superiors. Lyra wants to understand what shard transformation produces and use that understanding for the House's long-term advantage. These objectives are not perfectly aligned.

**The breaking point:** If the Shard Division's exposure becomes inevitable, Mira will cut it loose. She has been through this calculation. Lyra has been through the same calculation and has provisions — specifically, the transformed subjects and the data they represent would need to be moved, not destroyed, and the move requires infrastructure that only the Guild can provide. This mutual dependence is the leverage players can use if they understand it correctly.

## EXPOSURE MAP — What Needs to Happen to Take Either Organization Down

**Exposing the standard Guild operation:** Requires: Evidence connecting the Night Market's Guild distributors to Mira Voss (financial records, communication logs, a turned distributor). Effect: disrupts Lumencrest's substance distribution significantly, damages Guild's reputation, potentially forces new arrangement with city. Does not address the Shard Division.

**Exposing the Shard Division:** Requires: Finding the containment facility, documenting the subjects, connecting the operation to the Collector and through the Collector to Mira Voss and to Lyra Vael. Effect: criminal exposure for Mira Voss and the Collector, civil and criminal exposure for Lyra Vael, political exposure for Sevan Vael. Does not fully destroy the Guild — the standard operation can continue under new Lumencrest leadership.

**Exposing House Vael specifically:** Requires: Connecting Lyra to the Shard Division (direct financial records), connecting Sevan to Lyra's activities (more difficult — the deliberate ignorance is a real defense), and ideally the Aurum conversation about the conditional vote language (Cass Vorn holds this). Effect: political collapse of House Vael's Lumencrest position, Sevan's removal from Council, Lyra's prosecution. Does not address the Guild's standard operation.

**Full exposure:** All of the above. Possible. Requires players to be operating at maximum faction reputation in multiple districts simultaneously, to have built relationships with Coldwell, Demi Wren, Mira Ashton, and the Academic Quarter's key figures, and to understand the connections between the threads rather than treating them separately.

The consequence of full exposure: Lumencrest's political situation destabilizes significantly in the short term. The moderate voice on the Council (Sevan) is gone. The most effective criminal logistics network in the city is disrupted. The Manufactories' substance supply changes. And four transformed people in a warehouse need to be decided about.

It is the right thing to do. It is not easy. That is why it's the right thing.
