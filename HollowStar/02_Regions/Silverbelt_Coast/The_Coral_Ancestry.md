# The Coral — Playable Ancestry

**Region:** [[02_Regions/Silverbelt_Coast/Silverbelt_Coast_Index|Silverbelt Coast]]  
**Home:** Silverspan Reef, offshore Silverbelt Coast  
**Population:** ~3,000–4,000 within the reef; rare elsewhere  
**System:** Daggerheart

---

## WHO THEY ARE

The Coral are an amphibious sea people who live within Silverspan Reef — a miles-long underwater reef system that most surface-dwellers have never seen and many don't know exists. Their civilization is not hidden; it simply has no particular reason to surface. The reef is self-sufficient, comfortable, and alive in ways that above-ground places aren't.

Their buildings are living coral formations shaped over decades. Their halls grow. Their sense of home is inseparable from the reef's health. Reef-keepers, not reef-owners — The Coral view themselves as tenders of something larger than themselves, not possessors of it.

**Physically:** Amphibious, with gill slits along the neck that allow underwater breathing without restricting surface breathing. Skin tones run from deep blue-black through teal and grey-green to pale seafoam, often with bioluminescent patches along the jaw, shoulders, or forearms that brighten faintly in darkness or emotional intensity. Eyes adapted for both surface and deep-water vision — large irises, often gold, silver, or deep green. Build varies; most are lean and strong from a lifetime of swimming.

**Culturally:** Non-hierarchical, communal, deeply attentive. Decisions in reef communities are made by talking until everyone can live with the outcome — not by rank, not by vote, but by sustained conversation. A Coral character may find surface political structures baffling or quietly infuriating. They understand stewardship. They often don't understand ownership.

## A CORAL WHO HAS LEFT

Every Coral who ventures to the surface has left something behind. The reef community is close and self-sufficient — departing it for the wider world is a deliberate choice, not a default. When building a Coral character, the central question is not what abilities they have but *why they left* and *what that means to them.*

Common reasons: curiosity about the surface world that couldn't be satisfied from below. A specific search — for a person, a piece of knowledge, a resolution. A fracture within the reef community that made distance necessary. Being sent by the reef elders to investigate something that affects the reef from the surface side. Or simply a feeling, which The Coral consider a legitimate and sufficient reason.

Above water for extended periods, they don't suffer mechanically — but players can lean into the texture of absence. Coral characters who've been away from salt water for a long time tend to get quieter, more inward. A hand touching a river on the way past. Pausing near the sea. Not a penalty — just a signal of where they're from.

## DAGGERHEART MECHANICS

### Ancestry Feature — All Coral

**Amphibious**  
*Passive*

You breathe both air and water indefinitely and suffer no penalties from underwater environments — visibility, movement, pressure to reasonable depths. You swim at your full movement speed.

Additionally, you have an instinctive read of aquatic environments. When you enter a body of water for the first time, you may ask the GM one question about it — its current, what lives in it, its depth, or what it connects to — and receive an honest answer.

---

### Foundation Features — Choose One at Character Creation

**Reef-Reader**  
*Once per session, when you observe a place, creature, or person carefully for at least one minute*

You may ask the GM one question about what you're observing — its health, its intent, what it needs, or what it fears — and receive an honest answer. This represents your reef-trained attentiveness to the subtle signals that living things send when they're in distress, at ease, or concealing something.

*Works on: people, creatures, ecosystems, and communities. Does not work on objects or structures unless actively maintained by living things.*

---

**Living Architecture**  
*Once per long rest, when you have access to natural materials and at least 10 minutes (1 minute in water)*

You can shape and encourage natural growth — coral, wood, stone, earth — into a functional structure: a shelter, a barrier, a bridge, a concealed space. The structure is solid and lasts until deliberately destroyed or naturally deteriorated. Scale is practical, not monumental — a room, not a fortress.

*The reef grows into what it's tended toward. So does everything else.*

---

**Still Water**  
*Once per session, when an ally within Close range takes damage that would mark a Hit Point or Stress*

You may spend your Reaction to move to them and reduce the incoming damage by your Proficiency. If you are within arm's reach, you may instead clear one Stress from them — the steadiness of your presence gives them something to hold onto.

*Reef culture teaches that the strongest thing you can do for something damaged is remain present with it.*

## CHARACTER QUESTIONS

When building a Coral character, these are the questions that matter most:

- Why did you leave the reef, and do you intend to return?
- What does the surface world offer that the reef didn't or couldn't?
- Is there someone or something at the reef you're still responsible for, still in contact with, or still avoiding?
- How do you relate to surface concepts of ownership, borders, and hierarchy?
- What do you miss most about being underwater?

## PLAYING A CORAL AT THE TABLE

Coral characters tend to be observant and unhurried — a disposition from living in an environment where moving too fast disturbs things. They often notice what others miss. They are rarely the loudest person in a room, and frequently the most useful.

Their relationship to community and care is distinct from most surface ancestries. They may be confused by why surface people make decisions so quickly, or why rank determines outcomes rather than conversation. They may be unexpectedly calm in situations where others panic — or quietly overwhelmed by things that surface people have learned to ignore.

The bioluminescent patches are worth playing with. They respond to emotion — brightening in anger, fear, or deep focus; dimming or going cool in grief or suppressed feeling. Coral characters can try to control this, but it's the equivalent of controlling a blush. Other Coral can read it. Perceptive surface people notice.
