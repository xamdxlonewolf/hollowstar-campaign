# The Veleth — Marsh Sight

> *"Ask them how they knew. They'll say the water told them. Ask them what that means. They'll stop talking."*

---

## OVERVIEW

The Simiah of Canopy Run have lived above the Glittering Marshes for longer than any other ancestry has lived *in* it. Generations of building on branches that dip into the water, of tapping subsonic codes into wood that connects to root systems that connect to shard-rich mud — something changed in some of them. Not all. Not even most. But in certain individuals, the generational immersion produced something that has no precise name in Common.

The Simiah call it ***Veleth*** — loosely, "the underneath listening." Outsiders who learn of it at all call it Marsh Sight, though it has nothing to do with eyes.

It is not magic in any formal sense. It is not a spirit gift or a shard mutation. It is something biological that developed slowly over centuries in people who were already paying extraordinary attention to an environment that was already extraordinary. The shards didn't create it — but the shard-energy woven through the marsh's bioluminescent ecosystem gave it something rich to read, and the richness of that first environment shaped what the capacity became.

**The Veleth travels.** A Canopy Run Simiah who leaves the marsh doesn't lose it. They carry it wherever there is water — rivers, lakes, harbors, coastlines. The marsh is where it is sharpest and most familiar, but connected living water anywhere gives it something to work with. Only in truly dead or dry places — deep desert, high mountain stone, places where no living water runs — does it go quiet.

---

## WHAT IT IS

Every body of connected water is alive with movement, pressure, and the resonance of the creatures living in and around it. Most people feel none of this. A *Veleth* practitioner feels all of it — not as sound, not as sight, but as a kind of spatial texture. The water has a resting weight, a pattern of small signals that constitute "normal." Disturbances change the texture. Learning to read texture is the first skill. Reading it clearly across distance is the work of years. Pushing *back* through it is something rarer still.

In the Glittering Marshes specifically, the Hollostar shards beneath Deepsilt emit a slow pulse-rhythm energy — once every several minutes — that every bioluminescent organism in the marsh partially synchronizes with. The marsh is effectively one vast, faintly humming network. A practitioner raised in it learns to read this network the way others learn to read faces. Elsewhere, without the shard-pulse, the signal is quieter but still present. A lake has its own rhythm. A river has its own character. It takes time to learn a new body of water — days to weeks of immersion before meaningful signal comes through — but it comes.

**What it feels like to the user:** Not vision. Not hearing. The closest description most practitioners give is *weight* — the water has a resting weight to it, and things that change the weight become readable. A person wading. A boat hull. A school of fish turning together in alarm. Something large moving through a deep channel. At its most developed, the feeling is less like sensing individual disturbances and more like knowing the mood of a place — whether it is settled, watchful, frightened, or wrong.

## WHAT IT CAN DO

### Foundation — All Practitioners
- Awareness of significant movement in connected water within roughly **half a mile** in familiar water; less in unfamiliar water until the practitioner has spent time there
- A general sense of whether a body of water is "settled" or "disturbed" — whether something has recently passed, whether something is wrong, whether it is safe to enter
- Awareness of how creatures in the water are behaving — a sudden silence from fish, birds lifting from the surface, things moving away from a channel — these register as information before they're visible

### Developing — Intermediate Practitioners
- Distinguishing *what* is moving — a person wading feels different from a flatboat, which feels different from something large and unfamiliar. Experienced practitioners can often identify individuals they know well by the specific way they move through water.
- Reading the emotional state of water communities — not in words, but in the aggregate behavior of every living thing connected to the network. A harbor where everything is slightly tense. A river where the fish have been still for two days. A marsh channel where nothing has moved in a week.
- Range extends in familiar water to **two miles or more** with clarity; less in unfamiliar water, improving with time spent

### The Push — Advanced Practitioners
At a certain depth of development, *Veleth* becomes two-directional. A practitioner can send *back* through the network — not words, not images, but a felt signal that registers in water-connected creatures as instinct. Unease. The impulse to move. The sense that something is wrong in a direction they can't name.

**What this looks like in practice:**
- Fish vacate a channel before a flood arrives that the practitioner sensed building upstream
- A Ribbet fisher on a lake suddenly feels inexplicably uneasy and pulls their boat to shore an hour before a storm hits
- Every water bird on a harbor lifts simultaneously for no visible reason, thirty minutes before something dangerous arrives
- Creatures in a river system begin moving away from a threat the practitioner detected, without ever seeing it

The creatures and people who feel this don't know where it came from. They felt uneasy. They acted on instinct. They may never know why they're alive.

**The practitioner's experience:** The push is not effortless. It requires stillness, deliberate attention, and a kind of sustained generosity — you are giving the network something rather than taking from it. It cannot be done while distracted or frightened. It works best when the practitioner is calm and the water is familiar. It does not always work. Sometimes the signal doesn't carry. Sometimes it carries and nothing responds. The practitioner never knows for certain whether the warning reached anyone.

### The Ceiling — Legendary Practitioners (one or two in a generation)
At its most profound, the Veleth practitioner is no longer just reading a body of water — they are briefly *of* it. A legendary practitioner sitting at the edge of a large lake can feel the full living state of everything connected to it simultaneously — not in detail, but in totality. Every creature. Every disturbance. Every place where the pattern is wrong.

A push from a practitioner at this level doesn't reach a few nearby creatures. It reaches everything in a connected water system at once. Every fish in a river. Every creature drinking from a lake. An entire harbor going still and watchful simultaneously, with no visible cause.

This level of development is profoundly rare. The Glittering Marshes may have one practitioner at this level alive at any time. In the wider world, among Canopy Run Simiah who have left and developed elsewhere, it is even rarer. Those who reach it tend to become anchored to a place — a particular river, a lake, a coastline — not by obligation but because the depth of knowing one body of water becomes something they don't want to leave.

## WHAT IT CANNOT DO

**New water takes time.** Arriving somewhere unfamiliar, the practitioner starts from little — general sense of "settled or disturbed," nothing more. Days to weeks of time spent near and in the water before meaningful signal develops. This cannot be rushed. The water has to be learned.

**Dry places go silent.** Deep desert, high mountain stone, places with no living connected water — *Veleth* has nothing to read and goes quiet. Practitioners describe this as a specific kind of absence. Not painful. The feeling of a sense that should be receiving something and isn't. Some find it disorienting. Some find it peaceful. Most find it lonely.

**Clarity is never perfect.** Even developed practitioners are reading texture, not seeing clearly. You can tell something large is in the eastern channel. You cannot tell if it's a boat or a creature. You can tell the harbor is tense. You cannot tell why. Misreading is possible, especially in unfamiliar water or difficult conditions.

**Heavy disturbance creates noise.** Heavy rain, flooding, large storms — the signal becomes overwhelmed by surface noise. Big things only, no fine detail.

**The push doesn't guarantee response.** A warning sent through the network may not be felt. Even if felt, creatures may not act on it. The practitioner has no way to know whether their signal reached anyone or changed anything. This is perhaps the hardest thing about developing the ability fully — acting anonymously, without confirmation, trusting the network to carry what you gave it.

## CULTURAL ROLE

### Within Canopy Run

*Veleth* practitioners are not formally recognized as a class or caste — Canopy Run is too small and too practical for that. But everyone in the settlement knows who has it, and those individuals are quietly central to the community's safety and the wider marsh's functioning.

**The Listeners** — the informal name. Not a title. Just what people say: "Ask a Listener." The guide system and the Listeners work in quiet coordination. Guides won't take outsiders into the deep marsh without checking with a Listener first.

Roughly **one in fifteen to twenty** Canopy Run Simiah develops meaningful sensitivity. Of those, perhaps one in ten reaches intermediate level. Legendary practitioners — one or two in a generation — are quietly mythic within the community.

**Training:** Cannot be taught. Either the sensitivity develops or it doesn't, becoming apparent in childhood between ages 6 and 12. Children who show early signs are given more time in the branch network and more patient attention from existing Listeners. This accelerates what's already there. It cannot create what isn't.

**The ethos:** Listeners are not celebrated publicly. The protection they offer is anonymous by nature — if you announce that the warning came from you, you change what the warning was. The tradition has developed a quiet ethic of unnamed service. A Listener who pushed a warning through a river network and saved a village downstream will almost certainly never know it worked. This is considered correct, not tragic.

### In the Wider Marsh

The other marsh communities know *Veleth* exists. The Fungril of Deep Bloom have a parallel in their mycelial network — the two communities share observations about the marsh's state, comparing what each perceives through their separate systems. Neither fully understands what the other reads. Both find it useful.

The Ribbet of Deepsilt have a quiet arrangement: Listeners inform them when outsiders approach the shard cluster. The elders compensate with access to deeper fishing channels. Neither community discusses this openly with outsiders.

### The Wider World

Canopy Run Simiah who leave the marsh and develop *Veleth* elsewhere become something the marsh tradition doesn't have a word for — Listeners without a home network. Some find a river and stay. Some move constantly, learning new water everywhere they go and leaving quiet warnings in their wake. Communities along certain rivers have noticed, over generations, that disasters seem to come with slightly more warning than they should — animals behaving strangely, a general unease before the flood, birds lifting before the storm. No one connects this to a Simiah who passed through two seasons ago and spent a month sitting at the water's edge.

## THE CURRENT CRISIS

The shard pulse in the Glittering Marshes has been changing for approximately fourteen months — coinciding almost exactly with when the approaching star became visible in daylight.

The change is subtle enough that only experienced Listeners notice it. The rhythm isn't faster or slower. It's shifting in character. The best description senior Listener **Mist-of-Branches** (Common-folk Simiah, age 84) can offer: it used to feel like water settling. Now it feels like water waiting.

The Deepsilt elders have been informed. They have not shared this with any outside faction. The Somnarchs, the Seekers, Lumencrest — none of them know the largest shard concentration on the continent is responding to the approaching star in a way that has never been observed before. The Listeners noticed it a year ago.

This information has not left the marsh.

## FOR PLAYERS — PC SIMIAH FROM CANOPY RUN

A Simiah character from Canopy Run can begin play with *Veleth* sensitivity as a background trait — one of the one-in-fifteen for whom it developed in childhood. This is a genuine asset that travels with them and grows through play.

**What a PC starts with:**
- Foundation-level awareness in any connected living water — the settled/disturbed sense, significant movement within roughly half a mile in familiar water, less in new water
- The sense that the marsh pulse has been changing — they felt it before they left, and the unease of not knowing why travels with them
- An instinct toward water. When entering a new region, they are drawn to rivers, lakes, harbors — partly because the *Veleth* is richer there, partly because it's where they feel most oriented

**How it develops:**
The ability grows through immersion — time spent near and in water, attention paid to what the network is telling you. A PC who regularly engages with water environments, spends time learning new bodies of water, and practices the push will find their range, clarity, and signal strength increasing. This is narrative development tracked between the GM and player, not a mechanical unlock list.

**The push** emerges through play — typically after the PC has demonstrated the patience and care the tradition requires. It cannot be forced or trained toward directly. It arrives when the practitioner is ready, usually in a moment when they feel something terrible coming and find themselves reaching toward the network without deciding to.

**The ceiling** — legendary development — is a long-term campaign goal. A PC Simiah who commits to this path and reaches it becomes something genuinely rare: a person who can send a warning through an entire river system, quietly, anonymously, and walk away not knowing whether it helped. That is the destination. It is worth pursuing.

**Outside water:**
The silence in dry places is noticeable but not distressing. Some players will find it interesting to play — the specific absence of a sense that is usually present. In the Desert of Glass, on a high mountain pass, in Lumencrest's stone city center — the *Veleth* has nothing to read. The practitioner is, for once, just themselves.

**Discovery path (if not taken at character creation):**
A Simiah from Canopy Run who didn't know they had sensitivity may discover it on first return to the marsh after time away — or on first meaningful contact with a body of water elsewhere. The pulse, once you've been away and come back to it, is harder to ignore than it was when it was always there.
